For most of you, the monitor that came with your computer has served you well. Reliable and decent, there are few reasons to care about an upgrade. However, a quality monitor can make a night and day difference in your workflow, even if you have a laptop. From bigger screens and higher resolutions to built-in speakers, exact colors, and different viewing angles, there are many good reasons to look into a replacement.

Below are the more common terms that you will find when describing monitors. For a more detailed review, check out CNET's profile .

Resolution

The most common monitor resolution is 1024 768 pixels. Resolution broadly refers to the number of pixels that you can find on the screen - the more, the better. However, the ratio of height to width of the screen changes. This ratio is referred to as the "aspect ratio." "HD" for a monitor is considered to be in the range of 16:9. However, much of the distinction is up to personal taste and need.

Viewing Angle

Typically, monitors are flat and project light straight outward. This means that, depending on the monitor, you may not be able to see the screen clearly when looking at it at any angle outside of straight on. If you are constantly sitting, this low viewing angle should not matter. However, for collaborative work and graphics-based operations, wide viewing angles can be critical. The better viewing angle skew toward the more expensive monitors.

Refresh Rate

A monitor's screen must constantly update colors and pixels to display what you want to see. In high-demand settings (movies, games, and rendering), the refresh rate, how fast the screen updates, can dramatically impact how you see the material on screen. On average, you will typically find a refresh rate of 5ms. However, with more expensive monitors, you can find better rates.

Size

Size is entirely a personal choice. For some, three monitors linked together is the only setup that allows them their full workflow. For others, a 19" desktop monitor is all it takes. Find what works for you and experiment!

Color Range

Most monitors can produce an extremely wide mix of colors. Tearing through the small differences here is not crucial. However, if you work in graphics, you will want to seek out a higher-end monitor with an extremely wide color range and intense clarity. If your monitor cannot reproduce colors in the way that you expect, you will notice differences that can reduce the quality of your work.

LED, LCD, IPS, and PLS

Different needs call for different monitor screen designs. From liquid screens to tiny LED lights, a range of technologies can produce similar, but different forms of the same items. The crucial differences are between IPS and PLS, In-plane Switching and Plane-line switching. The former is known for amazing viewing angles and colors, but relatively high lag and refresh rates and poor blacks shading. The latter has wide viewing angles and produces great depth at low power, but does not render colors as well as an IPS monitor. Play to your tastes. Many rely on the same technologies.

HD

Monitors will sometimes come with the label "Full HD." While it does help to point out a high-resolution setup, the qualification is not a set standard. Anyone can label their work "HD." Pay close attention to the actual details - color range, resolution, refresh rate - and ignore flashy sales terms.

Retina

Apple has developed what it calls a "retina" screen. The term is actually a reference to the human eye, which is only capable of seeing so much details before individual items blur. The items blurring here are pixels: a retina screen's resolution is so high that the human eye cannot discern individual pixels on the screen. This makes the monitor highly desirable for those working in graphics and video industries.
