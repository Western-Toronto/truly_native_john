How to Successfully Kill Your Betta Fish

2:01 PM - June 17, 2013 by Adam Short

0 Comments | Click to Contribute

Have you ever gone through the painful process of having to say "goodbye" forever to your beloved, pet betta fish?

Maybe you tried and tried but you could not save your betta from falling ill and dieing long before his time.

Or, at the request of your distraught children, you conducted a sad, but meaningful ceremony explaining to them why you needed to flush poor "Goldie" down the toilet.

In any case, when our pet fish die too soon, it can feel like we lost a family member ...and we would do anything to keep them here with us for as long as humanly possible.

Do you want to kill your fish?

Of course not...but, by engaging in the following practices, your betta may never live to reach his first birthday...

1. Save a Few Dollars and dont get a Water Heater


What happens when your heater goes out in your house and it's 20 below outside? You call a repair man because you want to stay comfortable and avoid freezing in your own home. Well, bettas dont ask for much more from you; they would like their home to be the right temperature as well.

Their natural habitat is the warm climate specific to the Asian countries of Thailand, Vietnam and Cambodia, and this is the type of environment in which they thrive.

The water temperature in your betta's tank should be around 75 to 82 degrees Fahrenheit. Spend a couple of dollars and get a good water heater and make sure you have a thermometer to regulate the temperature of the tank as well.

2. Overfeed your Fish


If bettas were human, they would need to attend OA (overeaters anonymous) because they do not know when to STOP eating. They really like gobbling up those little treats of worms, insects and small crustaceans.

A betta should be fed about two times per day at a 2-minute feeding duration. While feeding, watch for belly expansion as this could be the sign of overfeeding . Your betta's belly is only as big as his eyes, so when it looks bloated, cut back feeding until his stomach returns to normal size. Overfeeding can also make the water in the tank cloudy and may cause a chemical imbalance.

3. Keep the Tank Dirty


Your Betta likes a clean home, so pay attention to left-over food particles and debris and sweep them up immediately. They can cause the tank to get cloudy.

When adding any dcor such as plastic plants or little figurines, rinse them thoroughly before adding them to the tank. The chemical residue on these items can be hazardous to your Betta.

A few pointers on keeping the tank clean:

- Never change all of the water at the same time
- Scrape algae from the inside of the glass
- Make sure the filter is working properly and it is cleaned regularly with water from the tank

4. Adding the Wrong Neighbors


Bettas do best with fish that are not top-of-tank swimmers, so mix it up a little with fish that are bottom swimmers.

Here are some good tank mates for your Betta:

- Guppies
- Platys
- African Dwarf Frogs
- Catfish (they can grow rather large which necessitates a larger tank)
- Ghost Shrimp

The following fish are NOT good neighbors for your betta:

- Angelfish
- Goldfish
- Mollies
- Georamas

Most importantly - do NOT place two male bettas in the same tank .Theyre very territorial. Good neighbors will keep your betta happy and alive.

5. Bettas in a Vase


Yes, your betta looks very pretty in the new vase you perched up on the window sill where the north wind blows inbut, by tomorrow you may be using the same vase for flowers.

Vases may seem to be a good idea for a single betta, but often, they do not have enough room at the top for your betta to swim comfortably. And bettas need the constant temperature and consistent, water-regulated (temp, pH, etc.) environment to live happily in their surroundings. Remember, your betta is a surface breather and likes to be at the top of the water.
