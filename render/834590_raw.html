The 5 Best Monosynths for First Timers

Posted by Trevor on Leave a Comment

When making the bold decision to take up a new instrument, often the most overwhelming part of the undertaking is choosing which model to buy first.But perhaps no task is more mind-boggling and complex than trying to pick your very first hardware synthesizer .

As electronic music has become more and more popular, this ritual has become a common and incredibly important one. There are decades worth of vintage technology floating around the secondhand markets on the internet and major brands have responded to this newfound interest by creating diverse new lines of synthesizers that are truly analog and come all shapes and sizes. With so many options it seems almost impossible to choose the right one for your needs.

But worry not. Were here to help you sift through all the rumors and the endless, often-contradictory user reviews about some of the most exciting and popular options out there and help you f ind that right first monosynth the one that has the highest quality, strongest build, the most flexibility, and the best sound while also giving you the most bang for your buck.

Analog, digital, and everywhere in between, lets take a look at some of the coolest and most powerful machines to cut your teeth on.

1. Arturia Microbrute


The newest creation from French audio company Arturia , the Microbrute is the new little brother to their previously successful beast, the MiniBrute . For those not familiar with the brutes, these powerful little synths are one of the best examples of a company thats trying to reintroduce the fat warmth of analog circuitry in an innovative, 21st century fashion . These powerful monosynths tout a 100% analog signal path and a bunch of unique features not found other instruments of this price range, including a fascinating patch system which gives this machine a modular synth edge, and the USB connectivity for computer based parameter editing. Snag it now for just $300 .

2. Dave Smith Evolver Desktop

This is the first synth made by Dave Smith since the selling of his famous company Sequential Circuits way back in the 80s. His brand, already a legend in the world of synthesizers, had to introduce something truly special to get the attention of young musicians used to trusting big names like Roland, Yamaha, and Korgand he did just that. There is nothing quite like the Evolver - it has the wonderful features you would want in a classic analog synth, but with tons of modern flair that just makes it one of the best values you can get in the synth market. A true Analog-Digital hybrid, Dave Smiths Evolver boasts 2 analog oscillators with classic Curtis low-pass filters, 2 digital waveshaping oscillators with samples from the classic Prophet VS synth, and as if that wasnt enough, a built in 16 x 4 analog style programmable sequencer! I could go on and on about how many features are crammed into this little thing and how incredible it sounds, but its probably best to just see for yourself . Oh, and theres no keyboard on the thing, so be prepared to have a MIDI controller handy!

3. Novation Bass Station II

The most recent edition of the classic Bass Station synth by Novation is probably one of the most underrated synths out there. The Bass Station II features all the incredible warmth and fatness of the original Bass Station but with plenty of new features and control, plus a great built in keyboard and mod/pitch wheels! Dont let the name fool you while nothing quite beats a Bass Station for making both sweet-sounding and face-meltingly aggressive bass sounds for your music, this flexible and well-designed analog synthesizer can produce everything from soaring leads to texturous morphing sounds . Some of the features of this powerhouse: 2l analog oscillators PLUS an analog sub oscillator for bone-shattering bass, storage for up to 128 patches, full analog style on board parameter control (great for learning the basics), built in arpeggiator and step style sequencer, and loads of built true analog effects, for biting and aggressive sounds!

4. Moog Minitaur


Not only has Moog been in the synthesizer business longer than anyone else, they have practically defined the sound of decades of music in all genres. Digital synths today try repeatedly to replicate the classic Moog sound to often limited success. And while Moog has been able to survive the changing musical landscapes and continue to produce quality analog gear, they are by no means affordable. Now Moog aims to provide an entry level synthesizer with the professional Moog attitude and sound in the TBP002 Minitaur. Pegged as another tabletop bass synth, this beauty can produce everything from crisp leads to growling, earth-shaking bass to sweet, warm R&B monosynth sounds. This thing has real analog VCOs with square and sawtooth waveforms, and real Moog ladder filter. Despite its name, this thing doesnt really sound like the original Moog Taurus. But if you are looking for an extremely pretty and well built, excellent sounding and easy to use tabletop synthesizer, look no further. With its big knobs and classic Moog layout, this synth is perfect to cut your teeth on or to add a huge, warm sounding synthesizer to your studio set up. This one is currently on sale at Amazon .

5. Korg MS-20 Mini

If you are a synth geek like me, you know that one of the most enduringly popular and famous monosynths of all time is the classic Korg MS-20 . A patchable, semi-modular synth that could route patches for unique, modulated sounds, and could even process external audio, the Korg MS-20 saw a huge revival when analog synthesizers become popular again in the 90s. Korg has now answered the continued call for a return to analog synths by releasing the MS-20 mini , a full recreation of the classic 70s synth, at 86% the size and with some fun new modern twists. Anyone looking to learn the basics of subtractive synthesis should consider this as their starting point, as its classic analog style knob control scheme and flexible patching abilities make not only an excellent instrument to learn on, but also possibly the last monosynth youll ever need . Not only does the MS-20 maintain its big brothers wonderful analog sound, it also carries on the endless modular flexibility that endows it with endless sound possibilities. While you cant save patches electronically, the classic control panel will teach you the fun of building sounds from scratch. It also comes along with new features like full midi and USB connectivity for computer based controls. Though its normally priced above $700, you can pick up a new one here for cheaper.
