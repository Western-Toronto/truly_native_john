How to Deal with Customers Who Have Financial Difficulties

By Elaine O'Gorman | Leave a comment | November 27, 2013
  • Tweet

Times are tough. Even though the U.S. economy appears to be slowly crawling out of the proverbial fire swamp, individuals and businesses are still hanging on for dear life and struggling with the financial repercussions of the past several years. Growth is slow. Businesses are still dying. The cost of my Venti Bold Coffee at Starbucks keeps going up by 10 cents. Its a jungle out there, which means some customers may have a tough time paying you.

If some of your customers are facing financial difficulties, here are some tips that will help you proceed with caution as you seek to get paid.

1. Watch for the early warning signs. Its rare that a customer will declare bankruptcy out of the blue. Its usually a slow, painful, and tragic decline. By watching for the early warning signs, youll get ahead of the problem and be one of the first in line for what might turn out to be their final payouts. Pay close attention to your customers payment habits. Take note if they start paying you lateespecially if they make late payments while also placing orders that are larger than normal. They might be stockpiling for the financial winter that is before them. Of course, a customer who starts placing smaller-than-normal orders should also be a red flag. Business might be dwindling. The sooner you identify a customer whos facing financial difficulties, the better chance youll have of getting paid before things get really ugly.

2. Be a friend. Working with customers who are facing tough financial times is more about customer service than collections. Jerks dont get paid. Jerks get ignored. Money problems are embarrassing, and they can feel extremely personal even to a business. Barging in and demanding payment will only shut down the negotiations. So you must be willing to meet your clients in the middle, hear them out, and try to understand exactly whats going on. Make a point of talking to them in person or over the phone, rather than just through email. Be a friend firstbe a human. Once you understand the situation, you can start working out a solutionwhether thats a negotiated settlement, a payment plan, or some other creative solution.

3. Be persistent. Being persistent is not the same thing as being pushy. Persistence means reminding your customer that you exist, not that he or she owes you money. Trust me, if a customer owes you money, she knows it. Reaching out on a regular basis to check up on her, touch base, or just shoot the breeze is a great way to keep youand your outstanding invoicetop of mind.

Yes, times are tough, but getting paid doesnt have to be. Approach your financially handicapped clients in a personable, helpful, yet persistent way and see if it doesnt get you paid.
