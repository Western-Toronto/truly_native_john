Paint with Light and dont be afraid of the dark

Take a good look at almost any great pre 20th century painting and you will see that light and dark are amongst the most powerful and evocative of the painters tools. Rembrandt, Turner, Caravaggio, all the masters create emphasis, drama, tension and meaning with light and shadow.

Great architecture and architects of the past do the same. The nameless genius that designed the Pantheon in Rome uses a great circular hole in the middle of the dome (an oculus) to send a piercing shaft of sunlight deep into the vast shadowed emptiness below. The beautiful details of the interior are picked out in the gloom by a spot of sunlight that moves across the surface during the day like a celestial searchlight. This, most stunning of Roman interior spaces, is defined by its darkness.

The majestic British cathedrals would be a shadow of themselves without the diluted stained light that washes the textured surfaces of the carved interiors. The result is powerful enough to create a little bit of smartly lit heaven on earth.

In an electricity free world, architects had to exploit low light levels to craft shadows because of the technologies available at the time. Windows were constrained in size due to the glass manufacturing process and artificial light was provided by oil burners or candles. They rose to this challenge and learnt to make the most of darkness turning a perceived weakness into a defining strength.

Fast-forward to the 20th Century and technology has moved on. We can now flood our buildings with electric light, and manufacturing techniques allow us to create windows of almost limitless size. The art of light and dark has been almost lost as increasingly our spaces are lit with a banal uniformity and sterility that flattens and removes any subtlety.

The tragedy is that many people think that this is modern architecture. A bright bland future that, in terms of lighting at least, resembles a high tech laboratory or corporate open plan office. Not the kind of place I want to spend much time, let alone live.

It is not modern architecture. It is boring, poor architecture.

Modern architects do create incredible spaces that manipulate light and dark with amazing effect. Peter Zumthor at the Spa in Vals and Corbusier at Chapel of Notre Dame du Haut in Ronchamp paint with light as a painter might. They create excitement, heighten your sense of movement through a building as you pass from light to darkness and back again. These masters of illumination are not afraid of the dark and can manipulate space with scale and emotion that make their buildings speak as powerfully and clearly as the master painters and architects of old.

At Ronchamp, a slither of light makes a massive curved concrete roof defy gravity and float. At Vals, pools of light lead the bather through the water pools of the dimly lit Spa like breadcrumbs in the woods. Swimming in the half-light transforms an often mundane experience into something sublime.

These ideas of contrasting light and dark have a core place in good architecture and while our homes are somewhat humbler than these exemplars, there are lots of lessons to be learnt.

The human eye is incredibly adaptable and sophisticated and our experience of light is relative. If you move from a very bright space to a normally lit one, it will feel gloomy. Similarly, if you pass from a dark room into a dimly lit one, it will appear bright. Transition spaces such as corridors and smaller anti-rooms can be used to create darkness that by contrast will make the adjacent rooms glow.

The usual response to gloomy spaces that dont get much natural light, is to try and brighten it up with colour or lights. This is not always the best approach and often ends up as architectural mutton dressed as lamb. Dont try and make it something it isnt, accept it is a dark space and then make it special.

With rooflights and artificial light, I tend to create pools of light rather than an even spread. Putting a rooflight or downlights adjacent to a wall lights the wall, bouncing the light around a room rather than simply lighting the floor. Sending light down a wall also highlights the colour and texture of a wall, use this to highlight materials and finishes.

Light can be used to emphasise the shape of a space. A rooflight in a tall space, such as above stairs, will accentuate its height and provides a moment of magic. A window or rooflight at the end of a corridor can emphasise its length. A pool of light in a dark space creates intimacy.

There is no reason to flood the entirety of our homes in brilliant light just because we can. It is often the sign of a lack of imagination rather than a bright brave future.

Dont be afraid of the dark, explore, celebrate and embrace it.
