SIMPLIFY
PERSONAL FINANCES

For simple personal finances, the single most important thing to understand is that over the past three decadesmany of ushave been living beyond our means andthat eventually causes complicationsthat undermine our efforts to simplify life. Even those of us who pay off our credit cards and save some money each month may be living beyond our means because of the hidden costs of life.

The large hidden costs of life:
  • House repairs that average about 1 to 4 percent of the house value per year (see WSJ article and US News article );
  • New car costs that total about twice the purchase price over the first five years (see the True Cost to Own calculator from Edmunds, Inc.);
  • Uninsured medical expenses the deductibles, co-payments, and uncovered expenses;
  • Divorce expenses -- even without alimony and child support, there will legal fees, thecosts of a second abode, moving fees, perhaps an additional car, and more insurance premiums. [Contributed by T. D.]
  • Lost income due to layoffs or extended illness that many people will experience at some time over their work lives;
  • Post-secondary education of children the minimum cost ofa four-year degree, except when on a rare full scholarship, is $50,000, and that assumes the kid lives at home;
  • Retirementexpenses are increasing because of new medical interventions and extended lifespans (see the Retirement page of this website); and
  • Long-term care about 70% of Americans who live beyond the age of 65 will need long-term care at some point, for an average of three years ( U.S. Govt statistics ).

There is wise debt and stupid debt. Wise debt involves borrowing to invest in something that is likely to increase our income or decrease our expenditures by more than the amount of the loan, origination fees,and interest. Examples of wise debt include borrowing for (a)professional training that we are well qualified for and in a field thathas high employment demand at wages well above our current level,or(b) to insulate our house so that we can reduce heating and air conditioning bills. Stupid debt is borrowing for personal pleasure that we cannot afford at the time, such asfor (c) a bigger television setor (d) a vacation. Much like heroin, stupid debt makespeople feel better in the short run and worse in the long runwhenthey have to pay off the loan in addition to paying all their other expenses.

If our expenditures exceed our income, there are only three permanent fixes: Increase income, reduce expenditures, or do some of both.Dealing with these realities is not simple, but it is less complicated than being deep in debt or retired with inadequate income.

Tips to Simplify Personal Finances

Suggestions for Increasing Income

1) Check out career options with the Occupational Outlook Handbook. This is the most comprehensive guide to occupations, indicating needed training, common career progressions, and employment prospects. It is a valuable guide for young adults, those considering mid-career changes to increase their earnings, and even those scouting second careers after retirement.

2) Consider engaging in further occupational or professional training. Community colleges are the best bargain in higher education. A portion of their students already have four-year undergraduate degrees but need marketable skills. Some private training institutes offer excellent training that is not available elsewhere but others are rip-offs that will leave you with no job and a large student loan debt. University masters degrees in professional fields can open doors to higher paying jobs. Warning: All postsecondary educational institutions, even public ones, employ nice representatives who seem sincerely interested in you but are paid specifically to get you to apply and/or enroll, and they are likely to be fired if they dont get enough people to do so. These nice peoplewill gladly arrange loans to cover most of your tuition and fees, but that will be stupid debt unless you can pay off the full amount and interest withina few years from increased earnings. Be realistic about your abilities and talk to potential employers about the quality of the program(s) that you are considering before committing to one. Going back to school could be one of the best or worst investments of your life.

3) Stretch yourself on your job. Arrange lunches with colleagues who earn more than you tounderstand what they do. Learn those skills on your own time, and volunteer to stay late and help when those colleagues are overburdened, out sick, or on vacation. If you handle your responsibilities well and prove you can handle higher level ones, you will increase your chances of promotion.

4) Max out any retirement contributions that your employer will match. The employercontributions and the tax deferral make this the best long-term investment that most people will ever make.

5) Put most of your retirement savings into broadly focused equity index funds. This is not only the simplest way to invest but has been proven to provide the highest net yield that most people can achieve. These funds include the Schwab S&P 500 Index Fund, the Vanguard Total Stock Market Index Fund, and the Fidelity Spartan International Index Fund.

Suggestions for Reducing Expenditures

6) Make a list of the above-mentioned hidden costs of life, tape itto your bathroom mirror, and scan it every morning. It should frighten you into moderating your daily discretionary expenditures.

7) Pay off all your credit cards as soon as possible and do not take out payday loans. These are the most expensive forms ofdebt in wide use. If you have two or more outstanding balances, financial logic suggests that you should first pay off the one with the highest interest rate and fees, but psychologically itcould be bestto start by paying offthe one with the smallest balance so you will have the satisfaction of that success to carry you through the long slog of getting out of debt.

8) Make your daily expenditures with cash anda debit card. Contrary to the popular delusion, money does not grow on credit cards. Limiting your daily expenditures to cash and a debit card will help prevent you from inadvertently shopping beyond your means. Occasional disciplined use of a credit card helps you establish good credit, and when the card has been carefully selected for its rebate program, it can save you a small percentage of the purchase costif, and only if, you pay off the full balance before interest charges apply. Many people soon fail to do that and are charged high interest on the outstanding balance.

9) Read Michelle Singletarys twice-a-week The Color of Money column, which runs in the Washington Post and 100 other newspapers. It is available online at www.Washingtonpost.com . She is the Oprah of personal financewise, humane, empathetic, funny, pragmatic, and tough-minded. The articles should boost your resolvefor gettingout of debt and maintaining personal financial stability.

10) Read the www.MrMoneyMustache.com blog by a guy who retired at 30 and dishes smarts and snark on how to do so without an inheritance or lotto jackpot. If you already have passed 30, his advice still could help you simplify your life, get out of debt, and retire earlier than otherwise would be possible.

11) Listen to the Dave Ramsey Show or read his book, The Total Money Makeover. He is part revivalist preacher and part financial counselor, who inspires his followers to become financially fit. Warning: Many financial experts think he overestimates the long-term financial returns likely to be achieved from stock investments and thus underestimates the portion of income that should be saved for retirement. [Contributed by Paul Kuns]

12) Cultivate inexpensive pleasures. Challenge yourself to find one free or inexpensive pleasure each month. Several examples are offered throughout this website. Then substitute those discoveries for your expensive pursuits.
