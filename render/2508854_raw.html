11 Weird NYC Subway Facts That Will Change Your Commute Forever

New York City
elyseromano May 29, 2014 0

New Yorkers have a love/hate relationship with the subway system. We love: that it can get us anywhere in the city for $2.50. We hate: that most of the time it runs like this . We love: that its an endless supply of eccentricity . We hate: all of these people . We love: hilarious Lord of the Rings-themed poster pranks . We hate: the G train.

Amidst all that emotional oscillation, its easy to lose sight of just how amazing our subway system actually is. The 23 lines make their way through NYCs 468 stations 24 hours a day, 7 days a week, 365 days a year. It is both one of the oldest and one of the largest subway systems on the planet, and ranks 7th in the world (with Tokyo at #1) for annual ridership. In 2013, the subway delivered over 1.71 billion rides. Thats even more rides than Miley Cyrus has taken on suspiciously phallic, larger-than-life food .

To celebrate our beloved (mostly) mass-transit system, weve scoured the Net for the weirdest, wackiest, and most wonderful NYC subway facts.

#1: Before the subway was the system we know and love today, there was a futuristic pneumatic train.

Image: NYCSubway.org

Way back in 1869, an inventor and wealthy businessman named Alfred Ely Beach came up with a radical idea: an underground system of circular, brick-lined tubes with high-powered fans at the ends. Air pressure from the fans would push a streetcar back and forth along the line, much like the pneumatic tubes that were once used to transport mail in New York City. Beach got permission from the infamous Boss Tweed to make the system a reality, but only as a means for moving mail, not people. Like a total boss, Beach and his crew built New Yorks first gas-free, soot-free, and steam-free human transportation system in secret. After two years, the surprise was unveiled to the public, but shortly afterwards Tweed set the construction of an elevated subway line in motion and Beachs experiment was demolished to make way for the subway we know today.

#2: Mayor George McClellan was the first person to drive a subway train in the completed system.

Image: CityClock.org/Wikimedia Commons

During his tenure as New York City Mayor, George McClellan oversaw the openings of the New York Public Library, Chelsea Piers, and Grand Central Terminal. He also licensed the first taxicab and christened the citys very first subway service. Originally only scheduled to ceremonially start up the engine of the debut subway train, Mayor McClellan liked the job so much that he asked to take the reins all the way up to Harlem. He piloted the new train to 103 rd Street before handing over the controls to George L. Morrison, the motor instructor of the company.

#3: The highest and lowest stations are in Gowanus and Washington Heights, respectively.

Image: Wikimedia Commons

At 180 feet below street level, the systems deepest station is the 191 st Street stop along the 1 line in Washington Heights. It is one of the few stations that still has full-time elevator operators , as elevators serve as the primary access to the platform. The highest train station is Smith and 9th Street (F and G) in Brooklyn, at 88 feet above street level.

#4: Michael Jackson filmed the music video for Bad on the abandoned platforms of the Hoyt-Schermerhorn stop.

http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dsUXAEzaC3Q

Video: michaeljacksonVEVO

In one of the most trilling moments in subway history, Michael Jackson, Martin Scorsese, and a young Wesley Snipes shot the acclaimed music video for Bad in the Hoyt-Schermerhorn station in 1987. In 2009, City Council member Letitia James petitioned the MTA to rename the station after the King of Pop, but the MTA told her to beat it. Despite the lack of an MJ plaque, Hoyt-Schermerhorn-Jackson lives on in our hearts.

#5: There was once a fleet of all-white trains.

Image: Wikimedia Commons

In a futile attempt to combat graffiti, the MTA introduced a pilot program of one dozen all-white 7 trains in September 1981. The Great White Fleet was inspired by a single white car that remained free of spray paint for a whole two months while parked in Queens (it was also protected by a chain-link fence and barbed wire, but obviously it was the paint job that did the trick). For some reason, Mayor Ed Koch thought the best way to prevent graffiti would be to turn the trains into giant, all-white roving canvases. You can imagine how that turned out.

#6: Theres a zoetrope that turns the subway into a movie machine in Brooklyn.

http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3IwVD5efXz0

Video: Bill Brand

Masstransiscope has been making commutes a bit more bearable since 1980, when artist Bill Brand installed the art piece toward the end of a Brooklyn tunnel that leads to the Manhattan Bridge. Brands installation turns the NYC mass-transit system into a colourful zoetrope, the earliest motion picture device, via a long slitted lightbox placed alongside the tracks with a series of paintings inside. As a train passes, the riders are treated to the illusion that the painting is moving. Keep an eye out for it next time you take a Manhattan-bound Q or B train out of the DeKalb Avenue station.

#7: Famed pop artist Roy Lichtenstein has a piece in the NYC subway system.

Image: Hard Seat Sleeper

Speaking of art, one of the most famous artists to put a piece in the New York City subway system is Roy Lichtenstein who, alongside Jasper Johns and Andy Warhol, was a leading figure of the pop art movement during the 1960s. Lichtenstein created the 53-foot-long, enamel-on-metal Times Square Mural in 1994 after being commissioned by the MTAs Arts for Transit program. The mural incorporates many of the artists signature elements, including comic book characters and science fiction themes, alongside New York City motifs. It can be found, appropriately, in the Times Square Station.

#8: Williamsburgs South 4th Street station isnt just abandoned it was never used at all, except by a secret underground art project.

Image: Vandalog

And still speaking of art Work began on the South 4 th Street station in Williamsburg in 1929, but the double blows of the Great Depression and World War II put its progress permanently on pause. It remained abandoned and unused until 2010, when The Underbelly Project illegally occupied the space (allegedly) and used it to showcase the works mostly painted directly on the walls, of course of 103 street artists from around the world. The show, which took 18 months to put together, is now commemorated in a book called We Own the Night .

#9: The Dead Mans Switch is not the name of a horror movie its an integral part of the subways safety system.

Image: m01229

Inside every motormans cab in the subway trains, there is a metal arm a few inches long called the controller. To set the train in motion, the operator must press down on the controller with several pounds of pressure, swivel it, and maintain contact. If the conductor lets go, for whatever reason, the emergency brakes are immediately applied. This is the macabrely named Dead Mans Switch. And yes, it has actually come in handy on a few occasions, most recently in 2010.

#10: The New York City subway lends its name to sporting terminology, mostly thanks to the Yankees.

Image: MTAPhotos

The term subway series refers to a series of Major League Baseball games in which the participating teams (and their fans) can travel to and from the venues entirely by subway. Historically, the term derives from the World Series games played between New York teams (in fact, the Yankees have appeared in all subway series games). A subway series is easily accomplished in New York because both Yankee Stadium and various other stadiums of the Yankees opponents (including Ebbets Field, Shea Stadium, and Citi Field) have been easily reachable via NYC subway.

#11: A 16-year-old once took control of an A train and successfully operated it for over 3 hours before he was noticed.

Image: The All-Nite Images

In a total baller move, a teenager named Keron Thomas posed as a New York City subway motorman named Regoberto Sabio on May 8, 1993 and proceeded to operate an A train for several hours. Before the heist, Thomas spent months reading books and operating manuals about the subway system, and even began getting into character by wearing an official blue motorman shirt while loitering on station platforms. Like a young Frank Abagnale (of Catch Me If You Can fame), Thomas lied his way behind the controls of an A train and successfully made every stop to the end of the line in Queens before being discovered. Apparently, he just really liked trains .
