15 Quick Tips for Starting Out on Twitter

Thursday, April 2, 2015


When you're just starting out on Twitter, it might all be a bit overwhelming, so here's 15 quick tips to help you.

You might not be aware of this, but people with really big accounts use sophisticated software to find who they want to follow and engage with. They use filters that analyze your profile to decide if they're going to follow you or not.

Even if they don't use software to analyze your profile, seasoned Twitter users will most likely not just follow you blindly, they will first take a good look at what you post and who you are.

How you post of Twitter is a big part of your online reputation, but if you follow the tips below, you will start to attract high quality followers in no time.

1. Make sure your tweets match your bio. It's okay to post something off-topic now and again, but stay focused most of the time.

2. Tweet at least 20 times before starting to reach out to others.

3. Add images to every third or fourth tweet, mix it with your personal comments, retweets and other content you find.

3. Get a proper profile image, get rid of the egg. Fill out your bio, add a web link.

4. Keep your follower/following ratio in check. Aim to follow slightly less than your follower number.

5. Don't follow spammy accounts.

6. Follow people that match your interests.

7. Thank people that follow you, even if you're not planning to follow them back.

8. Follow people back that follow you only if they match your target audience.

9. Thank people that 'favorite' your tweets, find something on their feed that you can 'favorite' back.

10. If you want a someone with a large following to follow you, first focus on #1 to #9 above, and wait until you have over 250 followers. Don't worry you will get there sooner than you think.

11. Use relevant hashtags, but don't use more than four in a tweet.

12. Try to keep your total tweet length under 100 characters, it looks better and leaves room for others to retweet, mention, and add hashtags.

13. Post daily if possible, but never let more than five days go by without a Tweet.

14. Watch the 'hashtag trends' bar on the left for hot topics that interest you, but make sure you understand what the hashtag is about before using it in a tweet.

15. Finally, if you have less than 250 followers, look for Twitter profiles with very high follower numbers, that follow just as many people back. They likely have an automated follow-back rule, and that will help you quickly get out of the starting blocks. This is not a long-term strategy, but will help you initially.

Have fun!!!

10 Best Ways to Improve Your Social Media Usage and Grow Your Business

Sunday, February 15, 2015

The other day I was asked if we have any advice on how to improve social media usage, which I thought was a really good question. I realized that although I talk about this a lot, there wasnt a specific blog post that addressed this succinctly. It took me a good week or so to get around to writing it up, but hopefully the next 10 points will give you some ideas on how to improve your social media usage, and grow your business at the same time.


1. Make it visceral. Double Dutch chocolate fudge cake with free ice-cream on the side.

Effective social media marketing and growing your business requires engaging and relevant content. Content is what gets people excited, it is what makes them share your content, and drives them to spread the news about your business through word of mouth.

Social media content sharing widens your audience to an entirely new world of fans, clients, and potential customers. It doesnt matter if you are a large business or a small entrepreneur, social media content is the most inexpensive and powerful marketing tool you can use.

Think about your content like you would think about approaching someone on the street. How can you best use 3 seconds to give them something that would be useful for them, and make them want to use your services or buy your products?

For a caf, bakery or restaurant, you could tell someone about your special for the day, but do it by showing them a fresh bagel, with a cup of espresso and beautiful latte art. Or tell them theres only one of your special double Dutch chocolate fudge delight cakes left today, and if they get a slice, it will be so fresh out of the oven, it will melt the free ice-cream on the side. If youre a plumber, landscaper or real estate agent, maybe give a good tip about how to improve your homes value for under $50.

Make it something concrete, give tangible, useful advice to make it irresistible, then keep on doing that as often as you can, at least once a day if possible.



2. Find new niche target markets. Get involved.

Facebook and Twitter have a combined 1.5 billion users globally. Social media gives your business more exposure well beyond your immediate geographical area. Cross country, state and national lines. Find new target markets. Get involved in online discussions.

Whatever your business does, there will be an audience for it on social media. Thats the beauty of online networking platforms where millions of people interact every day. Whether youre selling vintage jeans for $300, or candy with bugs in them, you will find a community that fits your business perfectly. Go and find your niche, and keep them engaged.

Remember that with social media, you have the power to speak directly to people that are interested to hear what you have to say. But you dont only talk to your established audience, you also get to talk to people that simply stumble upon your post, as well as people that did a search for a topic that matched your post. This combination of factors balloons your reach tremendously.

Even if you only have a couple hundred followers, your posts on social media can literally reach thousands of people. Dont get discouraged if you dont even have a 100 followers yet, just keep on posting relevant content to your niche, you will be amazed at how it can escalate.

3. Social media gives you amazing insights about your customers, use it to your advantage.

You have limitless opportunities to interact with potential customers and provide targeted information about your products, but you have to be clever about it. No longer are you limited to expensive generic print materials. The sky is the limit when you use social media like Pinterest or share pictures of your products via Instagram.

With print media, your best estimation of exposure comes from distribution numbers, but you dont know how many people actually saw your ad. With social media, you can get exact figures, which means that you can track the effectiveness of your campaigns with accuracy, and allocate your budget accordingly.

If youre willing to spend some money on advertising on social media, you can easily create a custom campaign that targets people that live close to your business, have spent money online in the last 60 days and are aged between 25 and 35, or any other demographic. The degree to which you can target your campaigns are astounding, and it gives you unprecedented control over who sees your ads.

Social media also gives you the unique opportunity to really interact with your customers, and potential customers. If you spend a bit of time, and I would suggest that you do, you can read up on what your followers are posting about. You can add to their conversations and truly interact in ways that would never have been possible with traditional media.




4. Bootstrap your marketing on a shoestring budget.

If youre just starting out, or even if your business is low on funds, you can substantially lower marketing expenses when you use social media. For every $1 you spend on marketing with social media, your returns have the potential of coming back 100x.

A typical small print display ad might cost you a couple hundred dollars per insertion, and be seen by a couple thousand people. With social media, it is possible to achieve the same result every day for free! It will still cost you in terms of the time you spend to curate or write the content, and create the images to go with it, but it is worth it.

The metrics that you are able to track on social media far exceeds any other traditional alternatives. Due to the highly targeted, laser-like focus you can achieve with paid social media campaigns, you can make sure that you get the highest possible return on your ad spend.
You can literally jump on Twitter, Facebook, Pinterest, Instagram and Google+, try out a new marketing campaign, and see what feedback you get without needing to spend a single dollar.



5. Listening to leads to customers [sic].

People make all sorts of comments on social media, and they often make comments on social media about services they need, products they want, or businesses they are not happy with. All of these communications provide you with excellent opportunities to engage with people. When you do, dont just post some canned response, take your time to craft a meaningful and thoughtful comment.

Posting on social media, is after all, about being social. If people wanted to read an advertisement, they, well, they probably work for an advertising agency :-) For the rest of us, please post something that shows that you care.

There are tons of online resources where you can set up little word-traps that catch phrases or keywords as they occur, and then turn your online eavesdropping into good advice by placing a well-timed post in response.

You might also find that people post some very negative things about some of your competitors, dont respond by turning it into a mud-slinging competition. Show sympathy and understanding for the problem, and propose a solution.

On the other hand, if youre on the receiving end of the negative comment, please dont insult your customer by posting a defensive response. Acknowledge that you understand how they feel, and make it clear that you want to keep them as a customer. Then get them to talk to you offline as quickly as possible. Remember, the world is literally watching, and even if you are right and the customer is wrong, the customer is always right especially on social media. The satisfaction you might get from proving that you are right will soon fade in the public backlash you will face.
When you respond properly to a negative review on social media, you can actually end up with a net positive effect and increase your online reputation.




6. Build loyalty. Gain trust. Sell value.

Build loyalty by consistently delivering on your promises with excellent customer focus. Loyalty is often misunderstood, you cant buy loyalty, and you cannot get it quickly, you have to work at it. You would not be surprised if your dishwasher worked every time you switched it on, and neither would you gasp in amazement if your rental car started up the first time you turned the key, those are your basic expectations.

Dont overlook the importance of basic expectations in your business, or your online activities. If you know a hotel chains beds are always comfortable, they always give you more than one type of pillow, and their breakfasts are always good, you are likely to be a loyal customer. If you get friendly, efficient service to go with that, it pushes your loyalty even higher. That is, until one of those basic expectations of yours get violated.

So how does that translate to posting on social media? Your online posts are like beacons that your customers follow to your business. Think about it this way, when youre walking along a path at night, and you have lanterns lighting your way at regular intervals, it creates a sense of security and reliability. The same with your online posts. A while back I was following one of my favorite Indian restaurants online, I didnt check in every day, but when I did, I would read through their posts and basically just follow the lanterns, and inevitably pick up the phone and place an order. Then one day, I noticed that they hadnt posted for almost two weeks. That was disconcerting, I wondered what had gone wrong, were they still in business? I immediately picked up the phone and called them, they assured me that everything was fine, and a month later they closed down.

I was a very loyal customer of theirs, their food was consistently awesome, their service was good, but their online posts was a sort of health-gauge to me. Your online posts serve as a signal to your customers, post consistently and you will not only build your brand, you will also build loyalty.

Trust goes hand-in-hand with loyalty, you gain trust one interaction at a time, by communicating truthfully and by keeping your promises. Lowes hardware gained my trust in a big way, when one of their associates pointed out that it would be better if I bought the cheaper option, because it would better match what I needed to do. It showed me that they were not just out to take my money from me, they actually wanted to help.

You can do the same with your social media posts, help your customers with insights, quick how-tos and honest, good advice. Once you have done all that, you can post the occasional sales pitch, because then you are selling value, based on loyalty and trust.

7. Think of social media as a way to build your brand.

There has been a lot of discussion around how to measure your Return On Investment (ROI) when it comes to Social Media. The more articles you read about the subject, the more you will come to realize that there are many different ways to measure the ROI of Social Media.

You might feel that the number of followers you gain is what you deem to be a return on your investment. Or, you might insist on counting how many customer interactions you have, or maybe you would only consider a sale to be a true measurement of success.

If you do any of the above, you will find that one month might be great, and the next dismal. The truth of the matter is, whether you gain followers, increase interaction with your customers, or get them to buy from you due to your efforts on social media, all of it builds your brand online.

You might not even make a single sale directly through social media, but by consistently posting relevant content to your target market, your business gets a voice which amplifies your brand.



8. Get real results in real time.

Everything you do, all your advertising, and analytics can be tracked in real time. Social media gives small and large businesses the opportunity to use advertising that is both timely and relevant. Current information gives your company a distinct edge. On social media, everything you do is in a public forum, and you can monitor everything your competitors are doing too.

The average Facebook post reaches 50% of its audience in 30 minutes, Twitter is known as the go-to social platform for any breaking news, and the feedback you get from a post can be immediate.
Use the immediacy and ephemeral nature of social platforms to your advantage to post about a flash sale, or to sell the last couple of items before you run out of stock. You can also keep tabs on which hashtags are trending, and as long as your post is relevant to the hashtag, chirp in to gain some additional exposure based on the trending topic.

9. What happens online doesnt have to stay online.

You can merge online promotions with offline events. People are increasingly using their phones to shop while they are in your shop, why not turn that to your advantage and post live tweets on an in-store online display. People will get a kick out of seeing their tweets ticker-taping across the screen, and you get them to interact with your brand in both the real-world as well as the online world.

Getting something like that to work is not nearly as difficult as it might seem, as all you really need to do is use a dedicated computer screen or even a tablet to display Tweets that mention your company.
You could even use an online post like a coupon, simply requiring the customer to show you on their mobile phone that they liked your page, or posted a Tweet, in exchange for a discount.



10. Pull Rank on Google.

By actively posting on social media platforms, you can improve your rankings on search engines. Search engine rankings, in turn, boost your visibility, which leads to more visitors and that leads to increased profits.

Google and Twitter have recently agreed to once again work together to bring Tweets directly to search results, this means that a Tweet that is relevant to whatever someone searched for, can literally jump ahead of other ranked web pages.

The benefit that this could bring to your business is that if you post consistently, your social media posts can have a big impact on your online visibility.

One of the best reasons to post on Google+ is for the benefit of your content getting picked up by Google. Your business profile on Google+ is also a great source of information for Google, and it plays an important part of how your content ranks online.

An interesting side benefit that you will get by posting on Google+, is that if anyone follows you on Google+, content that you post will be shown higher up in the search results. This is because when you search for something, your results are actually tailor-made, you can test this by sitting right next to someone and typing in the same search term on Google. You might be amazed to find that your results will not be identical, and in some cases, your results might be completely different to the person sitting right next to you.

You should also make good use of Google Hangouts, it is a great tool to host live interviews, show tips on how to fix something, or live-stream the results of a competition with bonus prizes for anyone that joins in on the Hangout. Its also great for simply talking to your customers, or for virtual meetings between multiple people.

Another reason to be active on Google+, is the communities that you get access to, and because there are fewer people here than on Facebook, what you post gets good exposure, and you can make a name for yourself in your niche. The super power that G+ has is the ability to post to specific groups, or circles, this means that you can target a specific group with a message that is relevant to them, without posting the same message to everyone.

Thats it! If you have ideas or suggestions that you would like to add, let me know, and Ill be delighted to add them to my next post.

What To Do If Your Twitter Account Got Suspended

Monday, December 29, 2014

Why Did My Account Get Suspended?

It's true, your Twitter account can be suspended by accident, but mostly it will only happen if you color outside the lines when it comes to the Twitter rules and best practices.

I had to help a frantic friend this morning, he logged into his account and got a message from Twitter saying that his account had been suspended.

The first thing I asked, was if he had bought any followers recently, he became a little defensive and although he didn't admit it immediately. Later he told me that he wanted to start the new year with a lot of new followers, so he bought some.

He followed one of the tempting Twitter bots that promised him thousands of followers in a day, paid for it, and was elated to get just over 2,000 new followers in three days.

My advice I had given to him previously, to not try and buy followers, but to simply post content each day had fallen on deaf ears, or at least it was temporarily drowned out by the tempting offer to quickly gain thousands of followers for as little as $5.

Now he was paying for it in ways he had not anticipated.

Get More Followers Fast! And Other Ways To Lose Your Account

The truth is that Twitter frowns upon both the act of paying for followers, as well as the aggressive following behavior that comes with a lot of bot accounts all of a sudden following your account.

If you really want bots, don't worry, they will follow you automatically in any case.

Some "follower programs" might require you to hand over your username and password, and then start following a lot of other people, in the hopes that they will follow you back. If they don't follow you back, they will unfollow the account, and continue the process over and over.

Twitter specifically prohibits this type of behavior and you should really never, ever, hand over your account details to a random person on the internet.

However, my friend had not handed over his account details, but he did pay for followers, so what went wrong?

From what I can tell, it looks like his account got suspended due to a lot of bot accounts suddenly following him. There are a lot of other rules that you need to be careful not to break, and you should really take the time to read through them carefully .

Fix It First

If you have paid for new followers, contact the person or organisation and ask them to stop immediately. If you have transgressed the Twitter rules in some other way, now would be a good time to stop doing that.

Unsuspend Your Twitter Account

Hopefully you might still be able to get your account back. Unless you had been particularly naughty, Twitter might allow you to unsuspend your account.

You might see a link in the body of the message from Twitter about your account being suspended, follow that link, get past the captcha, promise to stick to the rules, and you should have your account back.

You might not see your followers and following numbers for awhile, and you might lose some of them.

If you don't have that option, then you might need to go here https://support.twitter.com/forms/general?subtopic=suspended and plead your case - good luck!

If you have to resort to contacting Twitter, be nice, you won't get anywhere by being rude or demanding that your account be restored.

Overly Promotional Facebook Page Posts And The End Of Convertisements

Sunday, November 16, 2014

If you haven't seen the news yet, Facebook will be restricting " overly promotional " posts in news feeds beginning January 2015, but what does that mean for businesses that rely on Facebook posts to promote their products and services?

What Is "Overly Promotional"?

Let's firstly take a quick look at the type of posts that Facebook deem to be overly promotional, here's an example that they highlighted:


Here's another example, also directly from Facebook:

I must agree, these are the types of news feed items that I hate seeing.

The particular problem that they have highlighted is that these posts are not coming from ads. So these are not intended to be ads, from Facebook's perspective, at least. These posts come from business Pages that someone 'liked'.

The issue is therefore that posts that should have been pure news feed content that contribute to, and fit into the rest of the content, turns out to be pure ads for services or products.

Basically Facebook wants you to purchase ads if you want to post ads, and post content when you use the news feed.

Pay To Play

Clearly Facebook is drawing a line in the sand here, saying that posting ads should not be confused with posting news-worthy content.

It's fairly obvious that this change is going to hurt some businesses, but taking a longer view, it's also clear that polluting of the news feed by ads posing as content has become a problem for Facebook - I've started calling them "Convertisements". And it's a problem that could potentially drive people away from regular engagement if they did not do something.

If people start feeling that their news feeds are full of ads that they don't want to read, instead of content they deem to be entertaining, useful, news-worthy, helpful, insightful, thoughtful, heart-warming, and all the other reasons people keep coming back to check their news feed updates, it would kill Facebook.

Not Liking Likes

Does that sound too dramatic? Ever tried 'liking' everything in your Facebook feed just to see what would happen? Mat Hohan, writing for Wired.com did that and he found that his "News Feed took on an entirely new character in a surprisingly short amount of time. After checking in and liking a bunch of stuff over the course of an hour, there were no human beings in my feed anymore. It became about brands and messaging, rather than humans with messages."

Exactly the problem that Facebook is trying to address here. Facebook claims that they dug through a lot of data in order to come to their new insight, I believe it was a lot simpler than that. You don't need a data scientist to figure out that if you 'like' a post on Facebook, you will be fed even more of the same content.

Face-palm

So what is this, the end of the world as we know it? Not quite. At the end of the day, Facebook is a social platform supported by ads, not an advertising platform supported by social content.

So what should businesses do now? You should do what you should have been doing all along, engaging your customers on social media, and not trying to hijack their news feeds purely for the purpose of advertising your products and services.

Be Social

The whole point of social media is to, well, be social! In social sciences, the term reciprocity is used to describe the tendency for people to respond in kind. You could also call it the Golden Rule, and if you really wanted to get into discussing the multiple facets of this social norm, you could look into social capital as a framework for better understanding the underlying forces at work.

The implication is that by posting ads when people expect regular content, you are not doing yourself any favors. You have not contributed anything they deem valuable, and therefore there is no reason for people to reciprocate.

On the other hand, if you contribute something of value, if you give something that is deemed to be valuable by the person on the receiving end, you immediately stir up the basic human need to reciprocate in kind.

By giving something of value, you open the hand of the receiver.

Do Your Own Social Media Or Get Someone To Do It For You?

Sunday, September 21, 2014

With a name like Do-It-For-You Social Media Management, you might think that my immediate response would be to say that you need to get someone to do it for you. Not necessarily.

Do You Like DIY?

It might surprise you to hear that my first, off-the-cuff response, would be to do it yourself. The reward for doing social media for yourself, is similar to marketing and selling your own house, or doing your own garden work.

If you have the time and resources available, you will almost always get more personal satisfaction out of it, and get it done exactly how you want it.

No Free Lunch

If you're going to do your social media in-house, it is still not 'free'. Dedicating a person to handle your Twitter, Facebook, Yelp, Google+, Instagram and Pinterest accounts will mean that you have more control, but that person will still be dedicating close to 32 hours per month to one platform alone.

Looking To Hire?

Here are some pointers on what to look out for in the person that will be handling your accounts.

  1. Look for someone that has a natural ability to deal with customers, and that understands how a written message can be interpreted differently than the spoken word.
  2. The person should always react professionally, they should never ever argue with a customer, especially not on social media.
  3. Ideally you need a self-starter, social media is not your regular 9-5 job. At the same time, the person needs to be well organised and able to represent your company online, but also know when to escalate an issue to you.
  4. You need someone that is also creative, with at least a bit of a sense of humor.
If you're currently a sole proprietor, and you haven't had to deal with employee taxes until now, you would also have to be ready to handle payroll taxes.

I've come across an article by SurePayroll that has a great infographic, be sure to check it out: http://blog.surepayroll.com/calculating-payroll-taxes-101/



Training

Unless you're hiring someone with prior experience in dealing with a company account, do not make the mistake of thinking that anyone with a Twitter or Facebook account can just jump in immediately to manage your business' social media accounts. Allow them some time to settle in, and maybe attend some online courses to guide them in how a company account should be handled.

Software

These days you really cannot just use Twitter or Facebook directly to manage business accounts. You will need online tools like Hootsuite or Buffer to make it possible to manage all of the interactions, and keep track of posts.

Why Should You Consider Outsourcing Your Social Media Management

Handing your social media over to a social media expert means that you can put your focus back on running your business.

Do You Print Your Own Flyers?

An analogy might be the difference between running your own little printing shop that prints your flyers and articles, compared to paying a printer to do it for you.

In this day and age of social media, the company that specializes in doing social media management, is like that printing shop that prints and distributes your ads, flyers and coupons.

Costs

The hourly rates of most full-service marketing consultants can range between $75 and $150 per hour.

A full set-up and management service on one social network, that includes account setup, optimization, design, posting, etc. can typically run from $1,000 to $9,000 per month, depending on the platform and your company's needs.

A complete social media strategy that spans several platforms can cost you anywhere from $3,000 to $20,000 per month.

That's a lot of money.

Light At The End Of The Social Media Tunnel

The best solution might be for you to look at a solution that does not require you to spend the whole day on various social platforms, and also does not cost you an arm and a leg.

To do this, look for a company that can do all the heavy lifting for you, let them handle the daily postings on Twitter, Facebook and Google+, while you focus on occasional posts, done on your own time, as you have specific content to share.

This way you get the best of both worlds, you will be seen to be active on social media, but without spending all of your own time on updates, and you will also not be spending a fortune on it.

Impressive Impressions And Circulation

The amazing thing about social media is just how much exposure you can get from posting on platforms such as Facebook and Twitter.

If you don't have a lot of experience with social media, your best reference point might be placing an ad in your regional newspaper, classified or magazine.

Paying for a decent sized ad in a publication with circulation of 20,000 and a monthly print run, can easily cost over $200 per insertion.

Bear in mind that a circulation figure of 20,000 does not necessarily guarantee that 20,000 people saw your ad, and this is where things get interesting on social media.

Twitter has recently introduced more fine-grained analytics, that allow you to see exactly how many people saw your posts. Over the course of a month, with a relatively average account, you could easily reach 20,000 impressions.

The difference is that over the course of a month, you would have posted dozens of messages to current and potential customers. You would have collected new followers and interacted with people that are customers and potential customers, instead of just having a static piece of advertising.

Whether you own a large enterprise, run a medium-sizedbusiness, or even if you're your company's only employee, social media can make a big difference to your bottom line.

Goading Gallup And The Myth of Social Media

Thursday, August 7, 2014

The recent "Myth of Social Media" report by Gallup has caused quite a stir online. Numerous other reports say exactly the opposite of what Gallup appears to be saying, so who do we believe?

Let's start by looking at the Gallup report itself. From what I can gather, the data is based on a survey or group of surveys done betweenDecember 12, 2012 and January 22, 2013.

You might recall that in June 2013, Gallup reported that it "would adjust some of its survey methods over criticism for inaccurately forecasting that Republican Mitt Romney would win the 2012 presidential election." http://j.mp/V56b7e They surely would have had their 'A' team working on the presidential campaign, and that went so disastrous that they had to get experts from the University of Michigan to come teach them how to do it right. http://j.mp/1yh9Coq

This might also explain why it took Gallup a good 18 months or so to finally bring out this report. They had to wait for the dust to settle, hope that people forget that their survey methodology, and the way in which they report their findings, were found to be faulty.

However, let's just take it at face value and go with their findings. The report itself actually gives us some very valuable insights, which they gloss over.

Here's a little snippet of their report - you can find the full version here: http://j.mp/1sBbKqi


So the big reveal here is that 94% of us use social media to connect with family and friends.

That's a bit like saying 94% of us use roads to drive home. I don't know what their actual survey question looked like, but replacing it with our example of road usage, it might have looked like this:

For what reason do you use roads the MOST:
A. Driving home or to family and friends
B. Driving to buy products or services from a business
C. Driving to find information about a business

It should be fairly obvious that people choose to interact socially with their family and friends first.

Social media platforms like Facebook, Twitter and Google+ are vehicles that provide us a means of connecting to other people, mostly, our friends and family, and yet, we find billboards and posters along the road.

According to this survey, 29% of the people that travel on this social highway with us, are there to "follow trends/ to find product reviews and information" - that's huge! A third of the people you find on social media do so to find product reviews and information, for what reason do you think people do that? They want to get information and reviews on things they want to BUY!

So based on this survey 29% of people that are on platforms like Twitter, Facebook, Google+, Yelp are there to gather information on businesses that they want to purchase products and services from.

Let's put that into perspective by making an unfair comparison to other advertising media, just for illustrative purposes. Now I might be wrong, but I honestly do not think that a third of people that watch TV, do so for the purpose of finding information about products and services. You will probably also find that 30% of people do not drive around for the sole purpose of looking at billboards and posters along the way.

What this does mean, is that social media is a very big factor for businesses.

In addition, Gallup reports that 20% of people on social media are there to comment on, or write reviews about products and services.

Wow! Where else do you get that? Where else do you get the ability to see what people think of a business or product, and get to talk back to them? Radio? No. TV? No. Billboards? No. Flyers? No. Coupons? No. You probably don't even get that on your own website or in-store. Even if you ask someone to their face, they are probably more likely to speak their mind in the privacy of their homes by posting a review or making a comment on a social media platform. Gallup, please do a survey of that!

Let's move on to Gallup's statement on the influence of social media on purchasing decisions. The best way to do this is to just look at their own report.


What they're telling us here is that even though 29% of people use social media for the purpose of finding product reviews and information, and 20% of people do so to comment on and write reviews of products and services, 62% of people are not swayed by anything they read on social media platforms.

Let's construct a little example and say that we have a 100 people that read this review (this is an actual review):
You know what I love about this place???? I never have to come back! I never have to pay way too much money for food and service that suck, ever again. [...] If giving zero stars were an option, I would have. If electroshock therapy could make me forget I'd sign up. If I had a want I'd terminate your entire staff and management.
Or what about this one:
DISGUSTING. Creepy. Gross. I have to agree and say I would rather eat a tire.
ZERO STARS.
If we go by Gallup's findings, out of the 100 people that read these reviews, 62% will still go ahead and take their families to this restaurant for a birthday celebration, compared to another restaurant with only rave reviews.

The one piece of their research that I do agree with is that the number of 'Likes' a business has does not necessarily correlate to social media success.

That's not the whole story though, and as they say themselves, consumers are far more likely to turn to friends and family memberswhen seeking advice about companies, brands, products, or services - and then remember that 94% of people use social media to connect with friends and family.

Here's another nugget of wisdom from their report with regards to the financial services industry:
79% of customers visit the branch lobby for transactions and other services, while 11% of customers follow their bank on Facebook and 5% follow their bank on Twitter
Maybe it's just me, but I think it makes total sense that 79% of customers would want to visit bank for transactions or services, heck, a lot of transactions require you to show up in person! The 11% that follow their bank on Facebook and the 5% that follow them on Twitter is also not surprising - it's a BANK. Unless they have social media managers with some serious skills, I don't think a lot of people would want to follow a bank on social media. Sorry, BofA - incidentally, go do a search on Twitter for BofA and compare that to @Chase for instance. Lots of interesting information there.

Now let's turn that around, and let's say that one of their customers in that 5% that follow them on Twitter posts a scathing remark, about how they got ripped off on their mortgage by using this particular bank. The bank posts back and says there's nothing they can do, the customer should have read the fine print. What sort of impact would that have? All of a sudden the 5% of people on Twitter can bring them to their knees.

Before social media such a complaint by an individual would have been silenced behind the closed door of your bank manager's office. Social media amplifies the voice of the individual to the point where it can be heard by the whole world.

So, social media: not a game changer? You decide.
