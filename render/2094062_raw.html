There comes a moment in every new job when you have to have that really awkward "so, I'm like, living with a mental illness?" conversation. I have "clinical depression with agoraphobic anxiety," which is a bunch of fancy terms for some really common feelings. I'm a two-time survivor of suicide, and I've had a lot of jobs over the years, partly because my issues make long-term employment a challenge, but mostly because I work in bookstores and there's just a lot of turnover in the industry. I've realized that I've developed a routine for introducing people to this side of me, as I'm the jolliest depressed girl you'll ever meet and most people don't usually put it together on their own. So here's a little primer that might make a rough conversation go a little smoother.

The most important aspect of "coming out" about mental illness issues is timing. I like to wait until you're about a month in, so you're comfortable enough to handle awkward reactions but still new enough that it won't come across as a big secret you've been hiding from all your coworkers. I recommend testing the waters with a peer that has roughly the same position as you, just to gauge if you've picked the right moment, before sitting down with a boss. Does your coworker react with polite curiosity and a general attitude of "huh, that sucks"? Then the time is right. If they seem awkward, try to change the subject, or assume you're angling for more days off, too soon. If they're completely

Once I've determined that the time is ripe, my next focus is on tone. I usually go for a "here's a wacky fact about me!" attitude, complete with an arsenal of jokes, to ease people in. Especially when it's time to sit down with the boss, I also like take an "ounce of prevention" type approach, like I'm informing them of a peanut allergy. It's important to emphasize that this is a long-term condition that you've been handling for years (even if you're lying through your teeth) and not a new development. If you're medicated, say so. If you use other methods to manage your illness, give a few details. For me the most important thing is to come across as stable and reliable, to the point where I over-shared the first few times I attempted this conversation and weirded people out (if you start listing the names of medicines or yoga positions, you've gone too far!).

Now that the awkward coming out-ish period is over comes the "are you ok?" period. Well meaning coworkers like to check in anytime you seem out of sorts, attributing everything from PMS to a stubbed toe to your mental illness. They'll talk about their sister/cousin/friend who has depression/anxiety/autism and how such-and-such a tip totally helped them! Remember that you need to stay friends with these lovable idiots, so as tempting as it may be to bat your eyes and say "omg, I had nooooo idea chamomile tea can help anxiety, please talk about it for another twenty minutes!" you'll ultimately regret it. And it's important to remember that people have a natural I Can Top That instinct, so while it sounds like they're trying to compare their bout of adult chicken pox with your lifelong struggle, it's just another way of showing empathy. They don't realize they've made the conversation into a whose life sucks more contest, so trying to win is completely pointless. Despite the inherent frustrations, I've come to cherish this period in a working relationship because most people who are worth your time and effort will take the time to learn more about you as a person during these conversations and eventually become great allies.

Once things settle down and people seem comfortable with this news about you comes a dangerous period that I've only recently started recognizing. When you start a new job most people practice their best lifestyle in order to start themselves out on the right foot. It's common sense. "ok, now I can just be myself!" can lead you to stop practicing all those good habits that you spent the first couple months on the job building up, whether it be working out, eating breakfast, taking meds, anything that you had been actively focusing on doing everyday to help keep you healthy. You need to do you, but listening to your instincts and what your body is trying to tell you is not the same thing as cutting yourself slack. Just remember that while your boss may be aware of your issues and not fire you if they start surfacing at work, that should never be the everyday baseline of your working relationship. If you're just getting by, it's a sign that some part of your self care routine is broken, and no one can survive day to day like that for long. I'm not saying beat yourself up every time you hit the snooze button, but take a little time to reflect on how your day went everyday and attempt to head problems off at the pass.

Even with all these tips and tricks, I've found that it can take a long, long time for an illness to just become a normal part of your identity, and at some workplaces that may never happen at all. Here in the U.S. people haven't really been trained on what the appropriate way to talk about mental illness in the workplace is, whereas everyone's had to sit through a boring training lecture about how to avoid offensively sexual or racially prejudiced language.
