How to Avoid Getting Car Seats all Wrong

Published 19th February 2015 | By adam

Car seats have been hitting the headlines again recently, and not necessarily for positive reasons. The one thing this publicity does though is highlight the importance of a) choosing the right car seat for your child and b) using it correctly.

So how can you avoid getting car seats all wrong?

1) Choose the right car seat for the age of your child there are many different kinds of car seats on the market, so the choice can seem a little bewildering especially to first-time parents. The best way around this is to firstly choose a well-known brand such as, Britax so you can be sure the car seat meets the required safety standards. Secondly, make sure you know which car seat group applies to your child , so that you can choose a car seat that will safely accommodate their weight and size.

2) Be aware that forward facing car seats may put your child at more risk rear facing car seats are becoming more popular as research has proved that statistically your child is safer this way; as opposed to having them forward facing in a car seat, which could result in serious injury or even death. In a frontal collision the neck is subjected to high, concentrated forces which could be caused in a forward facing seat. Whereas a rearwards facing seat reduces these forces, because they are distributed over the back of the body and the head.

3) Ensure you fix the car seat correctly in your vehicle it is one thing choosing the right car seat, but another thing altogether ensuring that it is correctly fitted within your vehicle. That is why many parents nowadays look for the familiar ISOFIX anchoring technology or the latest European wide i-Size standard, to ensure that the car seat safely fits within their car.

4) Restrict the amount of time your child spends in a car seat recent research at Glasgow University, has discovered that strapping babies in one position in a car seat or buggy can hamper the development of their core strength and balance. This has also been supported by the work at the Institute of Education in London. Whilst we realise that further research will no doubt be carried out to verify this, it does raise a very valid point that we should avoid keeping our babies strapped into a car seat or pushchair for lengthy periods.

Remember that the law requires all children who travel in the front or rear of a car, van or goods vehicle should use a correct car seat until they are 12 years old or have reached the height of 135cm. After this they can use an adult seat belt.
