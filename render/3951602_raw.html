Future developer opportunities with the Microsoft HoloLens

10 March 2015

Asavin Wattanajantra , Editor, Microsoft Developers

Back to Articles

Virtual and augmented reality will be an incredible new playground for developers. We've already seen heralded introductions of Google Glass and Oculus Rift, and now Microsoft has entered the fray with its HoloLens.

The HoloLens is a fascinating new device which could be described as offering augmented reality with virtual 3D imaging through see-through, high-definition lenses, spatial sound and advanced sensors. This means that once you put on a HoloLens, you're provided experiences which allow you to enjoy virtual images and objects overlaid onto the real world.

As well as a built-in GPU and CPU, it has a third processor called a holographic processing unit, or HPU. This is what helps the headset understand where the user is looking, understand gestures, voices and spatially map the environment. In a sense it's like a natural extension of Kinect , that instead of tracking where you are, tracks what's around you.

Technology like this is nothing without applications though, and Microsoft has already been busy calling developers to look at making software that will run on the HoloLens. Every device running Windows 10 uses a common, unified core, so everything devs know about developing for Windows will help.

Those building on Windows 10 will know how to build for Microsoft HoloLens from a platform perspective. This is because it will be the first platform that supports holographic computing, with APIs that enable gesture and environmental understanding on an untethered device. On Windows 10 all holograms are Windows universal apps, while all Windows universal apps will work as holograms.

Developer Opportunities

What are the development possibilities? Obviously gaming is one, with Minecraft being a prime candidate for opportunities. Kids and adults alike can already imagine the possibilities of engaging with holographic objects through the lens, in the same way that a child may first start playing with a set of blocks.

Daniel Ernst, VR Developer at The Shoebox Diorama, told The Guardian , "Imagine a dad playing Minecraft with the kids - with augmented reality, he'd be able to interact directly with them and help build a castle. You could have a game of Dungeons and Dragons where your environment changes before your eyes at the whim of the Game Master.

"You could go on a quest around your home to find Pokmon, or play a real-time-strategy game where each player sees a "fog of war" on their opponent's side of the room. The opportunities are endless."

But AR and VR needn't be limited to gaming - commentators have already remarked and been shown industry and enterprise applications such as making a Skype 3D video conferencing call.

NASA developers and Microsoft have already teamed up to deliver software called OnSight, technology that enables scientists to work virtually on Mars. It means that scientists will be able to plan missions and science operations conducted with the Mars Curiosity rover.

"It gives our rover scientists the ability to walk around and explore Mars right from their offices," said Dave Lavery, program executive for the Mars Science Lab mission at NASA "It fundamentally changes our perception of Mars, and how we understand the Mars environment surrounding the rover."

OnSight uses holographic computing to overlay visual information and rover data into the user's field of view, surrounding them with images from the rover's Martian field site. The user can then stroll around the surface or crouch down to examine rocky outcrops from different angles.

Analyst Insight

James McQuivey, principle analyst and vice president at Forrester Research, told the Harvard Business Review that retail, travel, automotive and financial service applications were obvious fits for the HoloLens.

He used Swedish firm Ikea as an example, "If it built a holographic catalogue so that you can drop furniture into your bedroom and see what it would look like and walk around it, you know you have a game changer.

But the HoloLens was more likely to be useful in the enterprise. He said, "There will be holographic apps for technicians that do maintenance on jets on the tarmac, collaborative 3D environment for architects, and special headsets for dentists to guide them through tricky extractions."

At Microsoft Developers we've already seen the way 3D printing has been taken up in a big way. Adding virtual reality to this process could be a real attractive proposition to forward-thinking companies.

Andrew Martin, President at digital marketing agency Metia, said, "There's an undeniable intersection between 3D virtual reality and 3D on-demand printing. There's already talk about using technology such as HoloLens within R&D departments to rapidly prototype products, but it could also be used in the field by customers."

He also believed that developers should also look into creating applications that help business and consumer users to make better decisions through the visualisation of data.

He said, "As more activities are tracked and measured, and data sets are increasingly complex, there is an opportunity to add a third dimension to data visualisation that allows consumers to literally immerse themselves on a data set, exploring it from multiple angles.

"While the flat infographic has given way to animated data stories, the future could be a virtual data landscape where customers can explore data and create their own view on it."

Developer Opportunities

If you're a developer thinking of getting started, the best way to get familiar with Windows 10 is by joining the Windows Insider Program .

And if you're lucky enough to have registered for Build 2015 at the end of April, you'll get tips on how to design and develop for HoloLens, get information on the SDK and meet members of the team.

But if you're not attending, not to worry - you'll be able to stream it or watch recorded keynote and session presentations after the event.

If you'd like more general tips on how to develop for VR, take a look at an article we published last year at Develop Conference which gives some tips on what to look for when developing VR software, particularly for gaming.
