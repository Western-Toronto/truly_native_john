All Articles Feature Tea Culture Tea Production Teas
04.02.2015by

Few things are more commonly associated with British life than tea. Perhaps the queen; though even then, tea with the queen is a concept people around the world will readily imagine.

But what does tea mean to the regular people of London? How does it fit in with their daily lives, what kinds of tea do they drink, and why, exactly, is tea such an iconic part of British culture?

I decided to meet some real Londoners from all walks and find out how tea enters their lives.

Barry, 36, Contractor

"I like it strong, with a lot of sugars"...You know the old stereotype of builders tea, lots of milk and plenty of sugar... -Barry

The first thing I do when I get in in the morning is have my assistant put on the kettle, says Barry, as he sips from a large mug with the emblem of a local football team on it. Hes a large man with a friendly laugh, who after years of bricklaying has become a manager for large teams of builders in the current construction boom of London.

I like it strong, with a lot of sugars, he laughs, typical, I know, but there you go. You know the old stereotype of builders tea, lots of milk and plenty of sugar. Im not fussed about the kind. We buy PG Tips at home, and I think here in the office, just regular tea. I dont go in for that fruity tooty stuff.

Brands such as PG Tips and Tetleys, black teas, mostly from India, are by far the most popular teas in the UK. They come in teabag form, and are sold cheaply in boxes containing up to 200 bags, available in every grocery store and supermarket. They have strong, dark flavours that are perfect for mixing with milk - the typical way teas are consumed in Britain.

I drink about three to five cups a day, I should say. Always have done. Most people drink tea from a young age here I reckon. Its just what you do. When you get a few people together at home, someonell put the kettle on. I ask Barry if he drinks tea at certain times of day. Not at all. I mean, Ill have one when I get to the office [Barry works from a trailer on the construction site] and after that I never know when Ill have a moment free to enjoy a cuppa. When I was bricklaying youd have one in the morning, especially if you had a fry-up [this is a British breakfast of fried eggs, sausage, and beans], then after your lunch, probably, and then when you got home from work. But its pretty much whenever you feel like it.

Barry sits back and thinks a long time when I ask him why tea is so popular in England. I dunno really. On the sites it can get quite cold, so the workers drink a lot just to stay warm. Other than that I cant really say, he laughs again, maybe were all just addicted!

Lizzy, 22, Student

"I like green teas because theyre good for you, but really I prefer flavoured green teas, or Monkey tea, over the regular kind." -Lizzy

I meet up for a quick chat with Lizzy, a student of Art History at the University of Westminster, in the East End. Were sitting in the Timberyard tearooms, a homely but colourful tea shop with a mouthwatering selection of cakes. I started drinking a lot more tea, and less coffee, when I learnt about the health benefits, she says. I think for a long time coffee was kind of taking over. Starbucks and Costa Coffees everywhere. Big cities have a rush, and coffee is very much a rush drink. Those places sell teas too, but people want to wake up, not chill out, in a place like London.

I ask her if its difficult to drink tea outside in London. It kind of is! For instance, I like green teas because theyre good for you, but really I prefer flavoured green teas, or Monkey tea, over the regular kind. I find them a little bitter. You can find specific types in a lot of regular shops, or organic food shops. I drink tea when I get up and have a moment before rushing off, or when Im home studying. The thing is, I spend a lot of time out and about, and you have to be in a pretty trendy area to find somewhere you can sit and enjoy a particular kind of tea.

I think its making a comeback though, Lizzy exclaims, gesturing around us at the elaborately decorated walls, with artisanal tea shops and such. Its kind of ironic, but I think a lot of it has to do with the influx of foreigners into London recently. They come here and say Where are all the teas?! I thought this was England! So theres a market there.

Margaret, 68, Retired publisher

"Essentially I select teas that complement my mood. Were I to wake up grumpy, for example, I might be disposed to drink an Indian tea, with milk; on a more brisk day I might choose a Gou Gu Nou or Cloud and Mist." -Margaret

Margarets incredibly expensive and beautiful West London home, with its elegant vintage furniture and delicate ornaments from around the world, is a million miles away from Barrys messy trailer in the middle of a busy construction site. Im reminded of him, however, when I ask her why Brits love tea so much. Ooh, well, its terribly addictive, isnt it?

With her children away, Margaret is free to enjoy her passions: War diaries, gossiping with her equally-wealthy neighbours, and tea. She shows me her extensive collection of teas with nonchalant pride, offering me the sculpted jars to sniff the aromas.

Tea is such an integral part of the British mentality. By that I mean that if you can enjoy tea, it indicates an appreciation of subtlety, and of restraint. I ask her what she thinks of coffee. Its extremely brash!

I tend to drink tea throughout the day. I believe I may go through two or three pots daily; this is shared with friends, of course. Its an abnormally large amount, you know, but Im more of an enthusiast. I ask her about the teas she drinks. Essentially I select teas that complement my mood. Were I to wake up grumpy, for example, I might be disposed to drink an Indian tea, with milk; on a more brisk day I might choose a Gou Gu Nou or Cloud and Mist.

I adore the selection of teas at the Goring Hotel, as well as the Grosvenor house. The Athenaeum, Royal Horseguards, and Sofitel are also wonderful places to take tea. Though I do love to buy and enjoy tea at home, I feel that to truly enjoy something, it must be partaken of in fitting surroundings.

As Margaret tells me stories of high society, I give in to my temptation to ask her if shes ever had tea with the queen. She replies, I prefer to say the Queen had tea with me.
