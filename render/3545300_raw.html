Improve Your Level of Service or Lose Your Business

June 6, 2013 BT Lets Talk 1

Guest blogger Ami Nahari, BT Global Services

People ask me if I like to travel. Well, I like meeting new people and visiting new cultures, and I like escaping my daily routine and going on vacation, but all of these usually involve going through airports, which I dont like at all.

I dislike queuing at a seemingly endless succession of security checks. Im not a particularly impatient person, but those queues project unmistakable ignorance, stupidity and inefficiency, delivered with a tyrannical attitude and utter disregard for the customers needs. The US Department of Homeland Security, each airport administration and individual airlines all collaborate to make the passengers travelling day seem like a short visit to purgatory.

Recently, I flew from New York to Toronto. My flight was scheduled to depart at 6:30 p.m. and arrive at 8:00 p.m. The chaos in the airport was unbearable, the desk attendant was as accessible as a New York taxi in a blizzard, flights were being cancelled left and right, and departure gates were being changed by the minute. Our plane finally left the gate at 7:00 p.m., and then taxied for more than an hour and a half until we were airborne at 8:30 p.m., to land in Toronto at 9:30 p.m. Many people missed their connecting flights and were forced to stay the night in the airport. Toronto was my destination but I, too, was delayed a while longer, as local immigration officials decided to take me through some additional inspection. Needless to say, my dinner plans had to be cancelled, and I spent the night attempting to fall asleep in my hotel room despite my stress and frustration.

Examined through service management standards, my flight experience failed every single metric: response time, resolution time, performance, availability and, above all, customer satisfaction. This happens for one simple reason: they dont care. And thats because we, the consumers, dont have alternatives. Which means the horror show is here to stay, possibly for our lifetime.

The objective of service level management (SLM) is to defy precisely that outcome. The process goal is to interface with the customer and ensure their satisfaction. SLM is a core ITIL process because it examines the actual quality of service as the customer perceives it and how happy the customer is with it. Happy is not an IT term and cannot be tracked by a monitoring tool, and that is why SLM is different from other processes.

You can have a service level agreement (SLA) report showing 100% scores for every objective, but if your customer satisfaction is lagging , you must start a new record in your continual service improvement (CSI) register and prioritize this item. Likewise, you may have an SLA report indicating warnings and even breaches in some of your objectives, but if your customers are happy, you must be doing something right.

To be a successful service level manager, you will need to be attentive to your customers needs, maintain complete and relevant documentation, and negotiate and track your SLAs to improve your process. In this publication I will show you how. I will take you step by step through the necessary action for successful implementation of SLM. I will help you to foresee coming challenges, and learn how to overcome them. I will also reveal the secrets of the trade, providing you with behind-the-scenes experiences, recorded by first-hand practitioners.

I mentioned earlier that the air travel nightmare requires the cooperation of three different entities security, airport and airline to produce and maintain its unusual level of customer stress and discomfort. But, unlike airline passengers, your customers have alternatives. If you want them to continue using you as their service provider, they must experience a positive service performance. You have no choice but to establish a best-practice SLM process. Your competition does.

Ami has recently written a book on the Secrets of Service Management, his book is available through TSO , priced 45. Secrets of Service Level Management is a practitioner guide on how to successfully implement an effective SLM process, how to take the theory of ITIL and convert it into practice, and how to plan and execute process improvement. Ami also explains what the challenges are, how to anticipate them and how to overcome them.

    It is a similar situation to BT renting telephone lines to other service providers. Because they are considered competition the end customer suffers in times of technical issues because BT are not responsible for them.

    So how should we measure customer satisfaction?
    In my view the touch system is best. Only at the points we control, the areas the end user/customer actually comes into contact with the services/areas we provide service.
    These are the areas in which we should shine..
    Everything else is down to integration and communication. The real service management of the business world.
