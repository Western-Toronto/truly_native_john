Painting with light

Oxfordshire-based artist Rod Craig writes about the importance of light in his watercolour paintings.

Rod Craig works with watercolours to create beautiful paintings of landscapes and cityscapes, capturing scenes at different times of day and in different weather conditions, so it goes without saying that light plays a key role in his work.

Yes, light is the single most important element in my work, says the artist. I am probably best known for creating dramatic and stormy skies leaden with rainclouds, woodland scenes with the early morning light breaking through the trees, and urban views with sparkling reflections on water surfaces.

Despite watercolour being a difficult technique to master, it is Rods preferred medium as its perfect for creating luminous and energetic light effects.

The light in the landscape changes continuously, he explains. Only watercolour allows an artist to work fast enough to capture the magic of the moment. This way of painting is both challenging and completely addictive for an artist. You have to react quickly; there is no time for reconsidering or overworking and this of course is what gives the spontaneity and energy to a good watercolour painting.

But light doesnt just play a role during the painting process; as Rod explains, even the finished painting can be affected by natural light: The transparent nature of the medium means that when we look at a finished watercolour, we are seeing light reflected back from the paper through the layers of paint in a similar way that we might view a stained glass window so paintings can look strikingly different under varying light conditions.

When a watercolourist paints light through leaves or behind the clouds that is exactly what we are seeing when we view the painting, continues Rod. We are literally painting with light watercolour possesses a fluidity and a transparency like no other medium.

To Rod, white paper (the paintings light source) is far more than just a blank canvas. It is like silence to a composer. It is a starting point from which to create mood and drama, darkness and shadow, leaving highlights and space to form rhythm and harmony. Without light we have only darkness.
