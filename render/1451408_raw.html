Avoiding Retirement Account Mistakes

E*TRADE Securities 1

Print

In this article, well explore some common mistakes that might occur as you save for retirement or make withdrawals from your retirement account. Understanding these common mistakes can help you make better decisions for your own retirement needs.

Savings Opportunities

If your employer offers a 401(k) or 403(b) plan, chances are your employer may also match your contributions up to a certain dollar amount or percentage of your income. Dont overlook this very important benefit! When contributing to your employers plan, you should consider contributing enough to earn your employers full match to take advantage of this benefit. For example, lets say your employer matches you dollar-for-dollar on the first 5% of your salary deferrals. By contributing at least 5% to your employers plan, you will earn the full matching amount. If you only contribute 3% of your salary, you are missing out on free money, since your employer is willing to match more. Over time, this could have a large impact on your nest egg.

Contributions to your workplace retirement plan are automatic since they are deducted from your paycheck. This feature simplifies your savings program, since you dont have to remember to send checks or to initiate contributions. In addition to saving in your employer sponsored plan, setting up an automatic investing plan into an IRA is easy and it helps reduce the temptation to skip making contributions in favor of other less important expenses. Heres how the Automatic Investment Plan at E*TRADE works:

  • Choose a no-load, no transaction-fee mutual fund, an investment amount, and a savings interval. For IRAs, many people prefer to divide the annual contribution limit of $5,500 by 12 and invest $458.33 per month. Even if your budget wont allow you to make a full contribution, consider setting up a small recurring contribution plan that you are comfortable with.
  • Funds are automatically transferred in the amount you choose--on the schedule you choosefrom your savings or money market account. You can even request to transfer funds from your non-IRA brokerage account at E*TRADE to your IRA.

If you are over age 50, dont overlook the higher contribution limits allowed for IRAs and 401(k)s. In the year you turn age 50, you are eligible to contribute $6,500 to an IRA, which is an additional $1,000 over the regular contribution limit of $5,500. Also, for 2015, you are able to make salary deferrals up to $24,000 to your 401(k), which is an additional $6,000 over the regular contribution limit of $18,000.

Regular savings contributions add up over time, so by taking advantage of employer matches, automatic contributions, and increased limits as you get older, you may achieve higher account balances when you are ready to retire.

Retirement Plan Withdrawals

If you change jobs, you may have various options for your previous employer retirement plan account. Be sure to review all options available to you before deciding to make a rollover including keeping your assets in your former employer plan, rolling over to a new employer plan, cashing out of the plan, or rolling over to an IRA. If you choose to roll your account over to an IRA, you may have the option to have the check made payable directly to the financial institution holding your IRA, a process called a direct rollover. A distribution check made payable to you may result in a mandatory federal tax withholding of 20%, causing your gross distribution check to be reduced. If you had intended to rollover the funds to an IRA, you may have to add funds from another source equal to the 20% withheld by the plan administrator. Also, if you are under age 59 , you will be required to pay a 10% penalty on the amount withheld. When you file your income tax return, you should be able to account for the 20% withholding, but you may have a longer wait for the credit.

If the distribution from your employer's retirement plan includes company stock, carefully consider your options before you roll over the entire amount into an IRA. Transferring shares of company stock to a taxable brokerage account may be a better option than rolling them over to an IRA. This strategy is called net unrealized appreciation (NUA). NUA is the difference between the price paid for the shares (or cost basis) and the stocks current price. Heres how it works: withdraw company stock in the form of shares, not cash, and deposit them in a regular brokerage account instead of an IRA. Although youll have to pay ordinary income tax on the cost basis (and a 10% early withdrawal penalty if youre under age 59), the NUA portion of the stock is not subject to taxation until the shares are sold, at which point it is subject to the favorable long-term capital gains tax, regardless of holding period. This may provide a significant tax advantage if your company stock has appreciated between the date you originally received the shares and the time you take your distribution. If the appreciated stock was rolled into an IRA, the entire value would eventually be taxed as ordinary income upon withdrawal, which might result in the appreciation being taxed at a higher rate.

If you retired early, you may be taking withdrawals under a method called Substantially Equal Periodic Payments (SEPP). This method allows you to avoid the 10% early withdrawal penalty prior to age 59 . Distributions must be taken no less frequently than annually for five years, or until you turn age 59 , whichever comes later . Make sure you know the rules regarding your SEPP so you dont inadvertently cancel it, and owe retroactive IRS penalties and interest. This may happen if any of the following occur:

  • Modifications are made to the withdrawal method (only a one-time change to the life expectancy withdrawal method is acceptable);
  • Additional distributions are made over and above the agreed-upon withdrawals;
  • Any amount that should have been withdrawn was not;
  • Contributions are made into the account.

Also, if you have retired early and you are between the ages of 55 59 , consider your options before automatically rolling over your former employers plan into an IRA. Your former employers plan may allow penalty-free withdrawals after age 55 if you have separated from employment. These withdrawals may not be subject to the 10% early withdrawal penalty, a feature not available in IRAs, which dont allow for penalty-free withdrawals until after age 59 .

Once you reach age 70 , remember to request the annual required minimum distribution, also called an RMD. IRS regulations require that you begin taking mandatory distributions from your IRA or employers retirement plan once you reach age 70 . The first required withdrawal deadline is April 1st following the year you turn age 70 , then a distribution must be removed by December 31st of each year thereafter. Failure to take your annual required distribution comes with a hefty 50% penalty on the amount that should have been withdrawn.

Estate Planning

Neglecting to update the beneficiary designation on an IRA can cause problems for loved ones after the death of the original account holder. If there are no beneficiaries on file, the payout generally defaults to the account holders spouse, if married. If there is no spouse, the payout will may default to the estate. Dont underestimate the importance of updating your beneficiary information on file, especially if you have recently gone through an important life change, such as marriage or the birth of a child. IRAs are paid out according to the designated beneficiary on file. Dont let an unintended recipient end up receiving the assets youve worked hard to save.

If you are a beneficiary who has inherited a retirement plan, a common mistake is to request a lump sum withdrawal. Doing this may cause the amount distributed to you to be fully taxable, unless the original account was a Roth IRA or Roth 401(k). Consider transferring the funds to a Beneficiary IRA as a way to avoid immediate taxation. You are able to withdraw the funds as needed, or over your life expectancy, and potentially benefit from continued tax-advantaged growth.
