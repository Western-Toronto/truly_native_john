The Evolution of the Cell Phone

Posted by Nick Huang on 19 Jun 2013 11:31 with 0 Comments

The world's timeline looks a little different to cell phones. These highly prized productivity devices are now flourishing in the modern business and social environment. Since the earliest models weren't created until the mid-1950s, they have a warped view of history. Lets take a look at what time looks like from a cell phone's perspective.

The Precambrian Period: The 1950s


Image via Flickr by Qole Pejorian

Noteworthy development: Giant phones weighing 88 pounds come into being.

Unanswered questions: Did these giant phones spontaneously arise from an all-powerful designer? If so, why did he make a portable phone that few people could carry for more than a few feet?

The first "mobile" phone was introduced in 1956. Although quite perplexing, it shows an important development in the evolution of cellular phone technology . Until this point, phones had come with two distinct features: a handset (allowing users to transfer speech) and a base unit (allowing users to dial numbers).

The mobile phone combined these two features into a single unit. Many scientists believe it came in at least 18 colors. Some speculate that the range of colors may have played an evolutionary role, much in the same way that birds use plumage-displays to attract mates.

The Cambrian Explosion: The 1980s


Image via Flickr via Mikey

Noteworthy developments: Smaller, more efficient batteries made the phones portable

Unanswered question: Is this a result of punctuated equilibrium (an intense period of rapid evolution)? Or phyletic gradualism (during which evolution happens according to a more uniform timeline)?

Although mobile phones continued to evolve during the 1950s, 60s, and 70s, they remained unwieldy. Heavy batteries made it difficult for individuals to carry them for prolonged periods of time. It would have been easier to carry these early models in a backpack than a pocket.

A cell phone version of the Cambrian Explosion, however, occurred in the 1980s. During this time, several portable phones sprang into existence, although consumers looking to buy the rare species would have spent thousands of dollars.

That largely meant that the Cell Phone Cambrian Explosion benefited the wealthy, not to mention Zack Morris from "Saved by the Bell."

During the mid-80s, most portable phones still required large battery packs that stretched the definition of "portable." Those big boxes, however, solved a persistent problem. Cell phones had difficulty surviving for more than a few minutes when separated from an electrical source.

By the late '80s, however, several species had evolved smaller, more efficient batteries that let them explore the world.

The Middle Paleolithic: The 1990s


Image via Flickr by Guy Schmidt

Noteworthy developments: Phones become considerably more mobile and compact with longer battery-life and access to a larger network of cellular towers

Unanswered question: Were cell phones in the Middle Paleolithic Era ableto think abstractly and create art?

The Cell Phone Middle Paleolithic Era (that's the 1990s for humans) was a period of exciting change. By the beginning of the decade, phones had lost much of their heft, although they were still large according to modern standards. Many phones of the early '90s also had long antennas, which began to shrink over the next several years.

Perhaps most exciting, though, is that many cell phones shrunk enough to fit in pockets. This made them more portable than any communication device that had come before (other than pagers and notes written on pieces of paper, of course).

During this period, several manufacturers became interested in the possibilities of future cell phone evolution. While their research produced some monstrosities, history has judged them kindly, thanks to the prolific era of highly intelligent phones that followed.

Pre-Modern Era: The 2000s


Image via Flickr by Lemsipmatt

Noteworthy developments: Phones become even smaller and lighter. Batteries are more efficient. Prices fall dramatically, making them affordable for most people living in developed countries.

Unanswered question: Did Razr phones snap shut to protect themselves from predators that didn't leave behind fossilized remains?

In the early 2000s, Razr cell phones focused on functionality and aesthetics. Some scientists believe that the phones realized they could become more pervasive by making themselves increasingly attractive to humans. Instead of relying on the interests of the super-rich and ultra-cool, Razr phones developed traits that appealed to the average person.

Some advantages of Razr phones included:

  • Their small size
  • A flip design that protected the screen and made them more portable
  • A light weight that let them ride along with humans, practically unnoticed
  • Attractive screen graphics and expanded memory for contact information

Much like mitochondria, which was once a virus before invading cells, Razr phones found a way to become an essential part of humanity.

The Cenozoic Era: Now


Image via Flickr by LGEPR

Noteworthy developments: Phones evolve into small, intelligent computers that can connect to the Internet via cellular networks and WiFi.

Unanswered question: Will intelligent cell phones ever rise up and overthrow their human masters?

Today's cell phones have reached a whole new level of usability and intelligence. They have become so good at storing information and communicating with their users, that they're often referred to as "smartphones."

Part of the evolutionary trend has been for manufacturers to develop inexpensive cell phones that practically anyone can afford. Companies can then create and sell apps (tiny software applications) that give the cell phones amazing abilities. The battle for domination has been severe, with iOS, Android, Blackberry, and Windows operating systems competing head-to-head.

Only the strongest will survive.

A properly equipped, modern smartphone can:

  • Provide accurate directions to pre-determined locations
  • Play games with its human master
  • Communicate with other devices to share files and access the Internet
  • Use GPS capabilities to track moving positions
  • Play thousands of songs
  • Give human users access to websites

What Does the Future Hold for Phones and Humans?

We haven't reached the end of cell phone evolution. Currently iOS and Android devices compete against each other in a technological version of survival of the fittest.

iOS devices have the advantage of brand recognition that makes them more attractive to some consumers. Android phones, however, have evolved into lower-priced, hardware-agnostic devices that offer a wider selection of apps .

No one knows what factors will influence the future of phone and human evolution. If the past offers any evidence, though, the devices will become more integrated with human behavior. One day, they might even become a part of the human body!
