Emotiv Insight Mind-Reading Headset Helps Those with Special Needs

Innovative Technology

By Ken Kaplan, Intel iQ Managing Editor

Follow @kenekaplan

Cancel that standing monthly appointment with your neighborhood psychic because the age of mind-reading technology is finally upon us.

The Emotiv Insight is a wireless headset that met its $100,000 kickstarter goal on September 15 th . In fact it exceeded that goal. By about $1.5 million dollars. And it seems to be a testament to our societys readiness to use technologies that harness the power of our minds.

The headset itself is fairly straightforward: using 14 lightweight, built-in sensors, the headset reads and records the electrical activity in your brain and delivers that information to other devices using wireless bluetooth transmission. Whats truly mind bending is the multitude of applications this electrical information will power, and as the Emotiv Insight team has offered an SDK for any interested researchers and developers to push the potential for this device even further.

Currently, the Emotiv Insight is able to track your cognitive performance and your emotional health. It also essentially allows for mind control. Bluetooth-connecteddevices will respond to your mental commands such as push, pull, levitate, rotate, and even a command as complex as disappear.

What is perhaps the most exciting feature of such intimate neurological technology, though, is the potential to provide with special needs that ability to communicate and interact in new ways with the world around them.

In the case of a young woman named Cora, who was left in a semi-vegetative state after surviving a car accident at the age of 17, the Emotiv Insight has allowed her to begin directly controlling the outside world again for the first time in 13 years. So far, she has used the headset to control virtual objects on a computer screen, and even just that has provided an enormous amount of hope for Cora, her family, and all those who have been affected by such debilitation.

The implications of this technology are huge. Although Cora is currently only moving virtual objects, it is now quite possible that people living with disabilities will be speaking fluently, controlling programmed devices in the real world, and even creating art or writing music in the very near future.
