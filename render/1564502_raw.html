Business Process Outsourcing: the BPO value chain challenge

Business process outsourcing copyright fotoscool shutterstock

Organizations are confronted with multiple challenges across virtually all business functions and business processes. The changing priorities depend on the nature of the business and the market within which it operates.

However, some priorities are universal: optimizing processes, improving the customer experience (of a more demanding and empowered customer/citizen,/patient,/consumer), dealing with ever more data, digitization, driving more business and better connecting/integrating processes and stakeholders. These evolutions also impact why, when and how organizations outsource business processes and thus the business process outsourcing (BPO) industry.

Business process outsourcing isnt new: its broadly used and accepted by many organizations. Gone are the days that organizations wanted to do everything themselves, often as a way to show how big (as in how many workers) and thus how successful they were. Success is not measured by sheer size anymore. Its measured by outcomes and KPIs that are closely related with outcomes, business growth, innovation and customer satisfaction.

Evolving BPO dynamics and expectations: the digitization of business processes

In a very dynamic business landscape and with the growing role of digital transformation and communications, the role of business process outsourcing evolves as a consequence, with a more strategic role for BPOs (the outsourcing partners). Successful organizations today know what they need to keep in house, what they need to outsource and how they can connect both to achieve the best overall value for the business and the customer now and in the future as a goal.

So, while BPOs originally focused intensively on cost reduction and outsourcing processes that, when outsourced, free up new resources ( because with a BPO partner its simply cheaper and at least as good than to do it internally ), the BPO industry and outsourcing as such is dramatically evolving as well. From pure operations to business transformation and that same goal of value optimization (which doesnt mean that cost reduction and more traditional BPO tasks dont matter anymore, well on the contrary).

On top of that, pressured by among others very demanding customers, the need to accelerate the digitization of business processes is real, as McKinsey rightfully states . McKinsey gives the example of a European bank in the sidebar of its article Accelerating the digitization of business processes, explaining how the bank wants to digitize its top 20 processes. Other companies are doing the same and many will follow: look at existing key processes, prioritize how to improve them, launch process digitization programs and in that process determine what needs to be outsourced and what needs to stay in house.

Today, top business processes are indeed regularly outsourced. And, even if they are not, all processes get connected. So, to ensure that all these connected processes work the best way possible, expectations regarding BPO partners increase too. There is no more room for silos or disconnected processes.

Dissecting the BPO evolutions

The degree in which BPO changes, is very dependent on the vertical markets they serve, the range of services they offer and several internal and external factors.

But in general we see 1) the growing importance of value creation (with clear KPIs) beyond cost reduction (e.g. innovation, customer care optimization), 2) more hybrid models of (out)sourcing, 3) evolving partnership needs and models (and SLAs) and 4) other requirements in specific BPO segments.

Mutual value creation is key in vertical and horizontal BPO services

Value creation is key here: creating value for the business by creating value for the customers (and their customers). Even if, in general, most companies are loyal to their BPO partners, the need to create value across various value chains is a reality, depending on the scope of the services offered, whereby we typically distinguish between:

Vertical BPO services . Examples of processes that are typical to specific industries and thus require specialized outsourcing partners, knowing these industries, include typical processes in industries such as insurance, banking, healthcare, IT services, etc. Some examples: claims processing, health payer business process management, loan portfolio process management, etc. BPO exists in virtually all industries, even if the outsourced processes can vary from strictly operational to strategic.

Horizontal . Examples of business processes that are cross-industry and thus require a deep expertise of the specific process at hand, include HR business process outsourcing (from payroll to human capital management), Supply Chain Management (SCM) BPO, finance and accounting, document capturing, digitization of paper process and sources, contact center BPO, mailrooms and much more.

Three levels of BPOs

In practice both categories overlap (they can be mapped out on a matrix) and its in the degree and nature of outsourcing that we identify different layers of BPO companies:

1. End-to-end BPO

In the top layer, we typically find companies that are strategic partners with highly specialized end-to-end process optimization and management solutions. The companies in this segment are often involved in digital transformation and process digitization. They focus on the top organizations within the verticals they serve. However, many of them are also moving to the SME market to expand their operations. The companies offering BPO services in this segment are often one stop shops, focusing on (out)sourcing but also on solutions, services, consulting and more (implementation, staffing,). At the same time, many also work in a tailored way and de facto start with one or more services, even outside the context of a roadmap for future collaboration. From an (out)sourcing viewpoint, they go from project-based staffing to fully managed services and managed projects. This is where the so-called high-performance BPO see above- is positioned.

2. Niche and tailored

The middle layer most often specializes in a set of specific processes or industries and increasingly goes beyond the operational dimension. In a highly competitive landscape they tend to meet competitors from the top layer (who start to expand into the midmarket where some of the players in this middle layer are active) and now and then BPOs from the third layer (below) who are even more focused but look for expansion within their niche. Some want to move more towards the top level, at least in expanding their BPO services into more strategic areas. Offering tailored solutions and approaches is one of their areas of growth but thats also where they meet the top tier BPOs who are flexible enough to meet customer demands that dont require a one stop shop offering.

3. Highly specialized

At the bottom we find even more specialized players. They often are and remain focused on very specific tasks such as digitizing high volumes of papers. Some of the companies here look for expansion as well. However, they will typically look within their niche as mentioned. These BPOs have very strong traditional relationships with their customers due to their highly focused approach and often long-lasting relationships when the key focus was on cost reduction. Many want to keep that focus, even if the markets they operate in are often very competitive and margins erode. They look at technology as a way to differentiate themselves.

Some business process outsourcing services are offered by players in two or even each of the three segments. These services can range from pure operational tasks in the digitization of paper to case management and beyond.

Although some BPOs, specialized in very specific operational areas, prefer to stay in their field of activity and expertise as just mentioned, in general the pressure is on for BPOs to play a more strategic role and expand their offerings and relationships with their clients beyond the traditional cost reduction. And thats where we see most evolutions happening. It goes without saying that, given the role of digital nowadays, a lot of attention goes to technology as an enabler of business and customer value, both for their own operations and those of their customers.
