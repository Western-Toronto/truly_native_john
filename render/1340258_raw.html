Ana's Story: Living Healthier With Atrial Fibrillation

Living with atrial fibrillation means your life can change in a heartbeat. Here's how one woman revamped her health and her attitude.

By Diana K. Rodriguez
Medically reviewed by Niya Jones, MD, MPH

Ana Espinosa, 66, loves nothing more than a delicious gourmet meal and a nice glass of wine. In 2010, though, she started noticing that at night, when she was tired and full from an indulgent meal, she felt odd.

"I would feel palpitations," Espinosa says. "I could pinpoint these episodes to eating a big meal. But for years, I said it was just indigestion."

Although Espinosa suspected something might be wrong, it took more than 10 years and a serious scare for her to be spurred into action. "One day I woke up and my heart just went into persistent atrial fibrillation ," she says. "I had to go to the hospital, and that's when I was diagnosed."

Espinosa's condition was originally diagnosed as paroxysmal atrial fibrillation, which is an irregular heartbeat that occurs sporadically and stops on its own, usually within 24 hours but can last up to a week. However, after so many years without treatment, her paroxysmal atrial fibrillation turned into persistent atrial fibrillation, the kind of irregular heartbeat that persists longer than a week and might not stop without treatment.

The Emotional Toll of Living With Atrial Fibrillation

Espinosa experienced a range of emotions after her diagnosis of atrial fibrillation, which is also referred to as Afib. Doctors couldn't pinpoint a cause because she had no other heart problems, such as underlying heart disease. "Confusion, denial, fear I was stunned because I was a pretty healthy person until then," she says. "Once all that settled in and I got out of denial, I just plunged into doing research and finding out as much as I could about Afib."

Working with her doctors, Espinosa started making healthy lifestyle changes as she learned about managing atrial fibrillation. She started taking medications to slow and stabilize her heartbeat, and then underwent cardioversion, a treatment that sends an electrical shock to the heart to regulate its rhythm, but even that provided only temporary relief. Her Afib came back, and she underwent cardioversion several more times.

Ablation Does the Trick

After eight months of failing treatments and living with atrial fibrillation, Espinosa had a cardiac ablation, a procedure in which thin wires called electrodes are placed in the heart. The electrodes take measurements of the heart's electrical activity and destroy any damaged heart tissue that's causing the atrial fibrillation to occur.

Her procedure lasted seven hours and her recovery was lengthy and tough it took about six months to regain strength and have her heart rhythm stabilize.

But since the ablation, Espinosa says, her atrial fibrillation is gone. Although she no longer experiences an irregular heartbeat, she still focuses on a healthy lifestyle to prevent a recurrence. She no longer takes any medications except for a beta-blocker, which controls her migraine headaches, and she takes a variety of dietary supplements.

Espinosa no longer eats too much rich food either, focusing her diet on plant-based foods instead. She also walks about three miles a day through the hilly terrain surrounding her home in the Catskill Mountains of New York.

She strongly recommends that people struggling with managing atrial fibrillation take a look at their symptoms and create their own tracking logs to help keep Afib in check. "I think everyone should keep a diary so they can identify potential triggers and avoid them as best they can," she says.

Living Well With Atrial Fibrillation

It's absolutely possible for people with atrial fibrillation to manage the condition and live a long, healthy, and active life , says John Mandrola, MD, a cardiac electrophysiologist at Baptist Health in Louisville, Ky., and author of an atrial fibrillation blog . "Most people do get Afib under control and get back to normal with good treatment. They can have a very active lifestyle," he says.

Focusing on a healthy lifestyle, with four basic pillars, is the key to enjoying better health and atrial fibrillation management. "I compare it to the legs of a table," Dr. Mandrola says. According to him, these pillars are:

  • Good sleep
  • Good movement
  • Good nutrition
  • Good attitude

"If you do those four things, your heart rhythm and blood vessels will be better. There's a clear association between good sleep and good rhythm. The heart rhythm is smoother and the blood vessels are less inflamed," Mandrola says.

Enjoying Simple Pleasures

Being diagnosed with atrial fibrillation was a major wake-up call for Espinosa to take her health seriously and make changes. She still indulges every now and then in her favorite desserts, pizza, and wine, but she does so mindfully. "Atrial fibrillation can be caused by heart disease, but regardless of the underlying cause, it's important to do things that are good for your heart in general," she says. "But indulging moderately once in a while is also important for quality of life."

Since her atrial fibrillation diagnosis, Espinosa has also lost about 30 pounds, has more energy, and is far more focused on her health than she ever was. "I feel like I have a new heart," she says. Plus, she's become more focused on a peaceful lifestyle and on managing stress.

"The most important lifestyle change to help those with Afib or any other illness is a stress-reduction strategy," she says. "Mine includes meditation, breathing exercises, and finding meaningful ways to redirect the thoughts and emotions that create stress in my life."
