Asymptomatic Atrial Fibrillation: Should You Be Concerned?

By Sanjai Sinha, M.D.
Reviewed by Philip Green, MD, Assistant in Clinical Medicine, Division of Cardiology, Department of Medicine, Columbia University Medical Center, New York, New York

Take Note

  • Asymptomatic tachyarrhythmias in patients without atrial fibrillation are independently associated with a greater than twofold risk of ischemic stroke or systemic embolism.
  • Because asymptomatic atrial fibrillation is also associated with an increased risk of stroke, treatment is necessary to prevent embolic events.

Atrial fibrillation (AF), a well-established risk factor for thromboembolic events, especially ischemic stroke, can present many conundrums for management, in part due to diagnostic difficulty. Typical symptomatic presentation might include palpitations, dyspnea, and fatigue. However, AF may be asymptomatic, and thus subclinical, causing no alarm to doctor or patient. Even in patients with documented symptomatic AF, asymptomatic recurrences are common. 1

The first evidence of asymptomatic AF may be devastating: In the seminal Framingham study, among patients with stroke associated with AF, stroke was the first symptom of AF in 24%. 2 Paroxysmal atrial fibrillation (PAF), as opposed to permanent AF, is transient, infrequent, and often asymptomatic. In fact, up to 90% of PAF episodes may be asymptomatic. 1,2 This leads to delay in diagnosis. However, what do we know about morbidity and mortality associated with asymptomatic AF?

In PAF, one might postulate that long intervals of sinus rhythm, or even transient asymptomatic episodes of fibrillation, are less dangerous than sustained, permanent AF. However, the benefit of periodic sinus rhythm is likely outweighed by the increased risk of stroke that occurs during periods when AF converts to sinus rhythm. Indeed, several large trials reported no difference in the risk of stroke annually between those with PAF and permanent AF. 3,4 Prognostically, most cardiologists would agree that PAF poses significant cardioembolic stroke risk meriting treatment. This begs the question, if diagnosis is delayed due to the asymptomatic nature of the disease, what can be done to diagnose this disease earlier? Additionally, are there precursor states to any form of AF that might also be key to early diagnosis and treatment?

In a recent study, Healey and colleagues prospectively evaluated whether subclinical episodes of rapid atrial rate (>190 beats per minute for at least 6 minutes) detected by implanted devices were associated with an increased risk of ischemic stroke in patients who had no history of AF. They enrolled 2,580 patients over the age of 65 with a history of hypertension. 5 The detection of such episodes of rapid atrial rate correlates well with electrocardiographic documentation of AF. 6 At 3 months, 10% of patients in their sample had experienced subclinical atrial tachyarrhythmias, and at 2.5 years, a full third of patients had. The key finding of this study was that subclinical tachyarrhythmias were independently associated with a 2.5 times greater risk of ischemic stroke or systemic embolism, and that this risk was independent of other risk factors for stroke and the presence of clinical AF. 5

The results of this recent study are consistent with those of prior studies that identified an increased risk of stroke in patients with asymptomatic AF, and takes it a step further in detecting a possible precursor condition to AF. 1 Though these studies involved patients with pacemakers, and likely sinus node dysfunction, asymptomatic AF is very common in other elderly populations 7 While pacemakers are certainly not indicated merely to detect asymptomatic AF or rapid tachyarrhythmias, the optimal monitoring methodcontinuous telemetry, ambulatory electrocardiography, serial electrocardiography, transtelephonic ECG monitoring, or implantable loop recordersis uncertain, though longer durations of monitoring are likely to obtain the highest diagnostic yield. 8,9

Though the benefit of antithrombotic therapy in higher risk patients with clinical AF has reached consensus, this benefit has yet to be determined in patients with subclinical atrial tachyarrhythmias. Since 25% of strokes have no clear cause, and a proportion of these events are likely due to asymptomatic AF, 10 it is crucial to determine strategies to identify and treat this vulnerable population. Randomized trials of anticoagulant therapy in patients with subclinical tachyarrhythmias would be beneficial.

More on Atrial Fibrillation

  • Perioperative Atrial Fibrillation: Risk Factor for Ischemic Stroke

    Given the severe burden of ischemic stroke and the strong association between atrial fibrillation (AF) and adverse stroke outcomes, the prevalence of perioperative AF is of particular concern. Significantly associated with an increased risk of ischemic stroke following both cardiac and noncardiac surgery, perioperative AF poses a serious challenge to the postoperative management of surgical patients.

  • Rate Control in AF and Heart Failure: Is Slower Better?

    Among patients with heart failure (HF) and reduced ejection fraction, a lower resting heart rate is beneficial only in those in normal sinus rhythm, not in those with atrial fibrillation (AF).

  • Long-term AF Monitoring in Patients With Cryptogenic Stroke

    Two recent trials assessed the relationship between atrial fibrillation (AF) and cryptogenic stroke (CS) and the potential implications for disease management.

  • Atrial Fibrillation Linked to Cognitive Decline Independent of Stroke

    Recent research demonstrates a link between atrial fibrillation (AF) and cognitive decline, even in the absence of stroke.

  • Catheter Ablation in AF: First-line Therapy for Rhythm Control?

    Radiofrequency catheter ablation is a quickly evolving therapy that appears superior as a rhythm control strategy in selected AF patients.

  • Ablation in Atrial Fibrillation:
    Expert Interview with Steven Hao, MD

    From patient selection to a detailed description of the ablation process itself to discussions of success, durability, and safety, Steven Hao, MD, Medical Director of Electrophysiology at California Pacific Medical Center, shares his experience in the evolving field of catheter ablation for the management of atrial fibrillation.

  • WhatIf AnythingDo Fish and Omega-3 Fatty Acids Have to Do With Atrial Fibrillation?

    Observational data suggest that the association of omega-3 fatty acid intake and atrial fibrillation risk in the general population is U-shaped. Not all studies corroborate these findings, however.

  • Does Atrial Fibrillation Increase MI Risk?

    Atrial fibrillation is now recognized as an independent risk factor for myocardial infarction.

  • High PAC Count Predicts AF, May Improve Risk Model

    Premature atrial contractions (PACs) are largely benign heart arrhythmias that may trigger episodes of atrial fibrillation (AF). Now early research shows that PAC counts may predict AF risk in older patients.

  • Nonsteroidal Anti-inflammatory Drugs and Risk of Atrial Fibrillation: Is There a Relationship?

    NSAIDs may increase the risk of atrial fibrillation, a finding of particular relevance to elderly patients who frequently are prescribed NSAIDs for pain and inflammatory conditions.

  • Menopause and Hormone Therapy: A Possible Role in Womens Risk of Atrial Fibrillation

    Estrogen-alone therapy increased womens risk of atrial fibrillation compared with placebo, suggesting that hormone exposure plays a role in the development of arrhythmias in postmenopausal women.

  • Complementary and Alternative Approaches Relieve the Burden of Atrial Fibrillation

    Complementary and alternative therapies, such as yoga, meditation, acupuncture, therapeutic hypnosis, and tai chi, may be safely incorporated into the comprehensive management of atrial fibrillation.

  • Obesity Predicts Atrial Fibrillation in Younger Women

    The double epidemic of obesity and atrial fibrillation is cause for concern. Recognition of obesity as a risk factor for AF may offer advantages in early detection, primary prevention, and risk stratification.

  • Atrial Fibrillation Risk Is Low with Bisphosphonate Exposure

    Some unexpected data sparked concern that there is an increased risk of atrial fibrillation in osteoporosis patients using bisphosphonates. But most research indicates that the occurrence is rare.

  • A New Strategy for Managing Atrial Fibrillation

    Physiological mapping of the atrial tissues (rather than anatomical mapping) appears to result in shorter, more accurate, more efficacious ablative procedures to correct the chaos of atrial fibrillation.

  • Atrial Fibrillation and Dementia: An Association Beyond Stroke?

    Patients with atrial fibrillation experienced a 40% to 50% higher risk of dementia, irrespective of the presence of clinical stroke.

  • Gender Affects Stroke Risk for Atrial Fibrillation Patients

    Despite similar or better adherence to anticoagulant therapy, women with atrial fibrillation have an increased risk of stroke when compared to men, especially after age 75.

  • Non-Cardiac Risk Factors for the Development of AF

    Early management of underlying noncardiac disease processes may be important in suppressing the onset of AF.

  • Management of Atrial Fibrillation in the Heart Failure Patient

    Rate control is important in all patients with atrial fibrillation, but even more so in those with concomitant heart failure.

  • Paroxysmal Atrial Fibrillation: Diagnosis, Progression, and Stroke Risk

    Paroxysmal atrial fibrillation (AF) may seem innocuous due to its intermittent nature, but it may carry similar risks as permanent AF.

  • What's Driving Atrial Fibrillation Costs?

    Costs associated with atrial fibrillation are rising dramatically worldwide. Interventions that reduce hospitalizations may be key to reducing the burden.

  • Demographic Indications in Atrial Fibrillation

    Incidence and risks for atrial fibrillation vary widely from group to group. Awareness of these key differences may allow for a safer, more effective management approach.

  • The Danger of Non-Adherence in AF

    Established treatment guidelines are proven to reduce stroke in atrial fibrillation. New evidence reveals, however, that physicians and patients do not always comply.
