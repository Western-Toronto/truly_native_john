There are wires in LeBron's ads for Beats Wireless headphones

By Joe Kukura on Oct 27, 2014

You'll notice something curious in the all of LeBron James' online ads for the new Beats PowerBeats 2 Wireless headphones. The Beats PowerBeats 2 Wireless headphones have visible and obvious wires.

Take a look at LeBron James in the Beats Wireless headphone ads . Those headphones have wires - you can see them right behind James' ears.

Take a look at the new online commercial for PowerBeats 2 Wireless headphones , a commercial featuring LeBron James returning to his native hometown of Akron, Ohio. This moving and picturesque homage to James' return to northeast Ohio has racked up nearly 11 million YouTube views in less than a week online. (The ad has also had a limited television run in certain markets. Unsurprisingly, Cleveland is among these markets.)

But still, you can totally see wires on those Beats PowerBeats 2 Wireless headphones. Why the heck would anyone pay $200 for a pair of wireless headphones that clearly have wires?

According to Beats, the wires are not actually wires. Instead, they are "tangle-free wrap around cable". These are intended to keep your earbuds from slipping off when wearing your Beats PowerBeats 2 Wireless headphones during strenuous exercise like jogging, crossfit, or practicing basketball like LeBron James in the commercials. The intent here is that the Beats PowerBeats 2 Wireless headphones are intended to be athlete-friendly .

The "tangle-free wrap around cable" just really, really looks like wires. Depending on your semantic preferences, you might actually say they are wires. They do not conduct any electricity or signal, as the earbuds receive a Bluetooth signal from your mobile device or Wi-Fi-enabled mp3 player.

Beats even refers to them in some marketing materials as "wireless wrap-around cable." I'm still trying to get my head around the concept of "wireless cable."

LeBron James himself is an investor in Beats, and reportedly made quite a killing off Apple's recent purchase of Beats for $3 billion. According to ESPN , "Sources briefed on the situation say James realized a profit of more than $30 million in cash and stock in the Beats sale after he had struck a deal to get a small stake in the company at its inception in 2008 in exchange for promoting its high-end headphones."

So LeBron continues to do promotions for Beats headphones, a subsidiary of Apple. James also does promotions for Samsung -- Apple's main rival in the smartphone manufacturing sector. It's unusual for James to promote rival companies' products.

"[LeBron]has an incredibly strong relationship with Samsung, and he was one of the early partners of Beats, a representative for James told the New York Post . We see no issue with that going forward."

It does seem a little strange to be for James to be doing ads for two competing rivals. Almost as strange as a pair of wireless headphones that totally have wires.
