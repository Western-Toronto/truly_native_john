I am big

fat

girl

5 min read
  • CONFESSION /
  • PERSPECTIVE

I dont recall a time when I wasnt larger than all of my friends. I know anecdotally that I was a very small baby, and there are plenty of pre-school age photos of me floating about looking beach body ready, but in general I am usually big and my friends are usually small. I have a strong sense memory of realizing I was the only 4th grader wearing a bra, and to this day I have real nightmares about a moment from my childhood when my shirt rode up during a car ride to expose my stomach, and upon exiting the car my youngest brother screamed at me to put that away! No one wants to see that!

The fat has always felt inevitable to me, like the changing of the tide or the guy who digs through our recycling bin for cans. Just a fact of life. While I will every now and then purposely choose the healthy baked chicken, I havent ever really done anything drastic to try and change it. There was a single, miserable stunt at Weight Watchers in my teens that went so poorly I have only foggy memories of it. I do recall that it took place in a church basement, and that my mom drove me.

My mom was some kind of Grand Poo-Bah of Weight Watchers when I was young. She not only attended meetings faithfully, she also led them. I again have only hazy memories (if you havent noticed yet, my brain has the generous tendency to wipe out these uncomfortable memories. Middle school is just a blur.) but some of her old habits lived on in our family cuisine. The most

The one that has stuck with me the most is dipping potato chips in cottage cheese. It is incredibly delicious, especially with those flavored Kettle chips that are in every bodega now. My mom once opened up about how she used to eat apple slices sprinkled with Splenda as a meal. Until I left for college, the Diet Coke flowed like water.

Knowing my mom had a skinny past tortured me. I would open up her high school picture albums from the 70s and stare at her. Those long, thin legs poking out of her drill guard uniform! The flat stomach that even the bulky maxi dresses my grandmother made by hand couldnt hide; that stomach haunted me. Until quite recently, my mother and I looked nearly identical. We even had the same haircut for most of my childhood. We wore the same size, and would shop in the Womens Plus section at Nordstroms together, trips that are much less painful in my memory than I know they felt in reality. Mom would buy things at the Clinique counter that she didnt really need, just so I could have her promotional give-away make up bag with all the little samplers in it. Im sure right around here is where Im supposed to talk about my struggle with eating disorders and crash diets, or my addiction to exercise or even self harm. But I never did any of that. Like I said, my fat has always just been a part of me, unchangeable, like the color of my eyes or my birthmark that looks like Idaho. It has made me miserable, sure, and self-conscious, and has cost me a few relationships. I have despised the way I look, and loved it, and despised it again in a never ending circle to the point where I hardly notice Im even doing it anymore. But that doesnt make me fat, that just makes me a modern woman. I do struggle to be healthy, to eat right and exercise, but I work very hard to make sure I dont do any of that in order to be thin. Because right now my life is about art, and love, and finding the perfect black t-shirt, and making a better roast chicken, and dealing with the fact that Ill never meet Nora Ephron now, and teaching myself to play Dungeons and Dragons, and worrying about my dog, and finding a new job. And on those rare occasions when I have made being thin a goal in my life, it has become THE ONLY goal in my life. So I ignore the fat, and for the most part it ignores me. Like that weird smell in the fridge, I know Ill have to deal with it someday. I know that thanks to the fucked-upness of our society, life would be incredibly easier if I was a more normal weight. And I know that, no matter how hard I try, Ill never truly be one of those beautiful, fat and proud Fatshionistas. Because my mothers stomach still haunts me. The knowledge that, if I was willing to sacrifice my time, my habits, my happiness, and the majority of my life, I could be thin. Every now and then, I am forced to remember that my fat is not like the color of my eyes or my birthmark shaped like Idaho: my fat is me choosing happiness over thinness. And every now and then, I second-guess that choice.
