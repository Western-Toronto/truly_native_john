According to Henry James: "There are few hours in life more agreeable than the hour dedicated to the ceremony known as afternoon tea." We concur. Delicate sandwiches of ham, cream cheese and cucumber, and potted salmon; scones with strawberry jam and clotted cream; a variety of small pastries and of course, an excellent pot of tea compose this afternoon tradition. While only the most expensive places require patrons to dress for tea, it is fun to dress up anyway. Some of the best places for traditional afternoon tea are the main hotels--Savoy, Ritz, Brown's, The Langham as well as Harrod's department store. Any of the museums offer a casual and cheaper alternative, perhaps just a plate of scones and a pot of tea. The Victoria and Albert museum offers a self service afternoon tea in one of the magnificent William Morris rooms, or in the lovely courtyard. Take a break from sightseeing and enjoy this elegant and civilized period of relaxation all over London.

Five minutes walk apart, Spitalfields and Brick Lane have been much redeveloped but have managed to retain their charm. Brick Lane is still genuine East End London, with a combination of Bangladeshi curry houses, Jewish bagel shops, and Indian sari stores competing with designer bars and old-fashioned pubs. The market is chaotic and bustling-something between a junk heap and a goldmine for antiques and vintage clothing-but you have to look very carefully to find bargain Art Deco furniture or valuable designer cast-offs. A great stop-off for those in the know is the Sunday market in Old Truman Brewery; stuffed full of vintage, jewelry, and accessories. There are also some great food stalls and bars around, too. Otherwise head for the more rarified surroundings of the newly extended Spitalfields, on the site of the capital's old poultry market, now all glass roofs and designer delis selling tapenade and expensive olive oils as well as market stalls offering fresh produce. Gourmet travellers come for the excellent food at either John Torode's new venue The Luxe (he of the Michelin-starred St John in Farringdon) as well as Carluccio's, Canteen, and Patisserie Valerie-a veritable foodie paradise.

London Canal Museum tells the story of London's canals, which were once vital arteries of the capital's trade, and which today are green corridors of leisure through the city. In a waterside former ice warehouse, the museum's exhibitions also encompass the Victorian trade in natural ice formed by nature and imported using wind power and horse power to keep food and drink cool in 19th century summers. A huge ice well is on view to visitors. One of our exhibitions tells the story of the working horse in London, both on the waterways and the streets.
The museum offers an extensive education programme supporting the National Curriculum and there are occasional boat trips and guided towpath walks on the Regent's Canal. Visitors can go inside a traditional cabin, and learn about the cargoes, trade and people of the canals and the hard life they once used to lead. Silver certification from Green Tourism UK.

Churchill Museum and Cabinet War Rooms

View in Planner

1h30m
Address:
Clive Steps, King Charles St,
London SW1A 2AQ
United Kingdom
Opening Hours: 9:30 AM to 6 PM
Phone: +44 (0)20 7930 6961
Web Site
Price: 13 (10.3 USD) - 17 (13.5 USD)
Student Price: 12.80 (10.2 USD) , Show valid I.D.

Learn about Winston Churchill, the man who inspired Britain to victory over Germany in WWII at this innovative museum, employing all manner of multimedia to explain and inform about his life. The 15-meter-long interactive Lifeline contains more details of Winston's life than even his biggest fan could possibly want to know! In addition, artifacts such as his school reports, letters to his beloved nanny, and early writings provide an insight into this privileged, powerful, and charismatic politician. Then, step back in time to explore the secret underground nerve center of the country's war effort, still laid out just as it was abandoned after victory in 1945. Allowing Churchill and his team to keep working through the nightly air raids on London, the underground complex contains the map room and the Courtyard Suite, a series of rooms where the Prime Minister and his righthand men ate, slept, and even exercised, although it's highly unlikely that Winston ever used the gym facilities. Churchill's simple bedroom, where he held court in his dressing gown while smoking his morning cigar and enjoying many a glass of champagne, is also on display. There are frequent historical lectures and even the occasional 1940s' dance nights held in the Bunker.

Admission free

Laid out around Big Ben, its much lauded and photographed clock tower, the mock-Gothic Houses of Parliament occupy the site of the former Palace of Westminster and are one of the great icons of British (now slightly tarnished) democracy. Staring benignly down the river Thames, it's been the seat of government for hundreds of years, although what you see now is largely19th century. A devastating fire in 1834 destroyed the original palace, leaving only Westminster Hall. This medieval jewel has stood since 1097 and was the site of the trial of Guy Fawkes and his co-conspirators in the 1605 Gunpowder Plot to blow up Parliament. This is still celebrated with fireworks in England as Bonfire Night on November 5th. Today the democratically elected members of the House of Commons meet to govern the country and discuss their expenses, while being loosely monitored by peers in the House of Lords. The latter is mostly inhabited nowadays with financial backers of whichever party is in power. Guided tours of the Commons and Lords also include a trip up Big Ben. Form a line outside the Cromwell Green entrance for access to public debates.
