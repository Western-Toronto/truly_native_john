High-frequency trading hits online ad exchanges

By Joe Kukura on Nov 11, 2014

The latest get rich quick scheme in online advertising takes a page right out of Wall Street, or rather a page right out of Michael Lewis book Flash Boys . The same high-speed trading thats become common in stock markets and securities exchanges has become a cottage industry within the ad tech marketplace. High-frequency ad trading is a devious, bot-powered new practice of buying online ads at low prices on various ad exchanges then reselling them on other ad exchanges for higher prices. A clever high-speed ad trader can make tens of thousands of dollars in a single day manipulating the online ad market.

It's a 'get rich quick' scheme that gets its creators rich really quick. All of the buying, auctioning-off and reselling of online ads takes place in less than a 10th of a second.

The secret world of high-frequency online ad trading is explored in an excellent Bloomberg Business Week article " High-Speed ad traders profit by arbitraging your eyeballs ." Author Doni Bloomfield goes inside the anonymous underground of high-speed traders and talks to the industry experts trying to curtail them.

"I see a lot of guys who buy from one exchange, and they sell to another exchange, said one Harvard Business School professor who analyzes the online ad business. Some buy from an exchange and sell it right back to that very same exchange."

Think of these online ad exchanges like Priceline, showing a list of options and prices for a certain product. But the prices are bids, and the product is you an Entertainment section reader, a sneaker enthusiast or a potential political donor. Online ad exchanges track your online behavior and then auction off the ads that will display to you as the member of a certain reader demographic.

"Advertisers buy Internet spots in ad exchanges, where space is sold to the highest bidder," Ms. Bloomfield writes. "Even before ads reach the desired audience, they change hands in a complex volley of electronic trades between websites, ad space aggregators, exchanges, data analysts and ad agencies. Thats where the arbitragers come in. Using their own programs, they ferret out underpriced ads and resell them."

In other words, these high-speed traders employ bots whose job it is to find and exploit the inefficiencies of other bots. This could be what eventually leads to Cylons.

Or it could be a vibrant new field in the industry. Whenever one party is in the business of both buying and selling a certain product on a Priceline-style comparison shopping or auctioning platform, these days someone is going to develop algorithms to exploit tiny differences that pop up on the platform. The tiny differences add up to big bucks. We may end up embracing and accepting high-speed bot arbitrage as a part of the industry, and maybe these companies will come out of the shadows and turn up on a future LUMAscape .

Or to put it a different way... If you were offered an exciting new job with a high-frequency trading startup, would you say no?
