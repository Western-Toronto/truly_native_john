7 Tips for Coping With Embarrassing Crohn's Symptoms

Coping with Crohn's at home is hard enough, but dealing with embarrassing symptoms when you're in public requires a different level of preparation and planning.

By Beth W. Orenstein
Medically reviewed by Pat F. Bass III, MD, MPH
Print E-mail

Real-Life Tips From People With Crohn's

1
image
Passing gas during a meeting. Having diarrhea in a public restroom. Urgently needing to "go" in the middle of dinner with friends. These embarrassing symptoms go hand in hand with Crohn's disease .

But you shouldn't have to shy away from social engagements or walk into every meeting wondering whether you're going to be the center of attention for all the wrong reasons. We asked real people and their doctors about how they deal with the most embarrassing Crohn's symptoms. Here's their advice.

Passing Gas

2
image
Atlanta resident Natalie Rosenthal, now 37, was diagnosed with Crohn's when she was 9 years old. She says that coping with the condition is a challenge because you never know when you're going to have embarrassing Crohn's symptoms especially passing gas when out in public. When she was young and this started happening, she decided to laugh off the furious flatulence. "If you don't laugh, you'll cry," she says. After a while, her friends started laughing with her, not at her. "Humor isn't always easy to pull off, but it's good advice," says Douglas Wolf, MD, a gastroenterologist at Atlanta Gastroenterology Associates and a board member for the Georgia Crohn's and Colitis Foundation.

And although Rosenthal hasn't personally used them for dealing with excessive gas, a new line of odor-neutralizing underwear may be helpful. The underwear uses a carbon cloth that actually filters out smells potentially a smart idea for people with Crohn's.

Strange Stomach Sounds

3
image
When Christine Morris of Atlanta, now 26, was first diagnosed with Crohn's disease at age 16, she remembers sitting in class at school as her stomach made all kinds of weird noises. Other kids would find it hilarious, but for her it wasn't. Her tip for coping with this embarrassing Crohn's symptom: bulky clothes. "I wore a lot of sweatshirts when I was in school because the bulky material helped muffle the noise."

Having to Go at Inopportune Times

4
image
When therapist Frank Sileo, PhD, 46, of Ridgewood, N.J., accepts dinner invitations from friends, he worries that he may experience a Crohn's flare and suddenly have to head home to the comfort of his own bathroom. A common hurdle of coping with Crohn's is its unpredictability, says Peter Doyle Higgins, MD, PhD, a gastroenterologist at the University of Michigan Health System in Ann Arbor. To avoid ruining everyone else's time, Dr. Sileo's tip for coping with this embarrassing Crohn's symptom is to drive separately to social outings. "If you use your own vehicle, you can apologize, excuse yourself, and not feel so bad about leaving early."

Keeping a few emergency items on hand extra underwear, baby wipes, and deodorizer can also help ease your mind by being more prepared and able to remedy an accident if needed so you can stay at the social outing longer.

Leakage

5
image
Morris has had a number of colon and rectal surgeries during her 10 years with Crohn's disease. "As a result, I'm constantly leaking," she says. To deal with this embarrassing Crohn's symptom, Morris always wears panty liners. She also keeps what she calls a "Crohnie's kit" with her at all times: extra panty liners and underwear in a holder that's the size of a pencil pouch. It fits neatly in her purse so she has it with her wherever she goes.

Bathroom Noises

6
image
When you're in a public restroom, you'd rather avoid making the explosive cacophony of sounds that often accompany Crohn's symptoms, Morris says. One solution to this embarrassing Crohn's problem is to find alternatives to public restrooms. Often on her way home from church, she'll stop at her in-laws house nearby rather than use the church's facilities. Morris also likes the idea of smartphone apps that disguise toilet noises by making other sounds, like running water or the blowing of a hand dryer.

Diarrhea

7
image
When diarrhea strikes, a public restroom may be your only option and that means you may end up occupying the stall for what seems like an eternity when someone else wants to use it. Morris keeps anti-diarrheal medications on her at all times, just in case. "It can help with the diarrhea, gas, and bloating," she says. "And it provides relief pretty quickly." One caution: Don't start taking any medications without talking to your doctor first.

Still, public restrooms can be a lifesaver when you do need to go, and it's always a good idea to locate them as soon as you get to a new place.

Changes in Appearance

8
image
When Rosenthal took corticosteroids for her Crohn's disease during her teens, it would cause her face to look puffy an uncomfortable situation, especially when you're young. "It was difficult to walk the halls of school because people could see something was wrong," she says. For this and other embarrassing side effects or symptoms of Crohn's, Rosenthal relies on positive reinforcement from a strong support network, starting with her family. "My parents told me I was beautiful," she says, "and it helped me to stay healthy." She adds that it also helps to accept your disease and remind yourself that the embarrassing symptoms will pass.
