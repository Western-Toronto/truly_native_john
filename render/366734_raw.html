Pros and cons of employees working from home

John Argent
Executive Manager, Commercial Portfolio at Vero
Font size
a a a
April 08, 2015

The advent of technology has seen a well-documented increase in the demand for flexible employment arrangements.

More employees are insisting on a better work-life balance, even prioritising flexible arrangements over pay. Indeed, many Australian workers, such as carers (including parents), those with a disability or aged over 55, are entitled to request flexible working under the Fair Work Act 2009. It is up to the discretion of the employer you whether you wish to grant it.

This increasing demand for flexible employment means that providing options for employees to work from home is something that small businesses can no longer afford to ignore, or their employees might jump ship to a more flexible employer.

So, what are the benefits and risks of letting your employees work from their own homes? And is your business suited to having work-from-home employees?

The benefits

While the thought of leaving your employee to their own devices can often be daunting, you need not worry: research recently conducted by Stanford found that letting employees in this case, call centre employees work from home made them happier, less likely to quit and more productive.

The researchers note, however, that not all work types are well suited to working from home. The best roles appear to involve tasks with measurable outputs, such as call centre work, website development, word processing and so on.

Consider the following factors when determining if it is appropriate and suitable for an employee to work from home:

  • Is the employee the right fit to work from home? To be effective, employees working from home should demonstrate strong initiative in order to maintain contact and engagement with their team and other people they regularly work with.
  • Does the role generally involve face-to-face interaction with customers/clients, and if so, how will this be managed?
  • Does the role require a high degree of supervision or monitoring?
  • Does the role generally require a large amount of teamwork and daily face-to-face contact with other employees?
  • Does the role require coaching, training and/or supervision of others?
  • Will there be an impact and cost to the business, e.g. in setting up and maintaining a safe work environment at the employees home?

Dont worry if your employees need to meet face to face for team-work, creative projects or to develop innovations working from home is still possible. An ideal balance would be for your employees to spend three days per week in the office and two at home which is just what the Stanford researchers recommended.

Minimising the risks

Of course, letting your employees turn their home into a workplace brings risks, too so lets take a look at what you can do to minimise them.

The first thing to consider is Work Health and Safety issues. If your employee injures themselves while working from home, it counts as a workplace incident. Your Workers Compensation Insurance should cover this, but each case would be treated on its own merits. Consult your insurer for further information, if required.

As the employer, you need to ensure that your employees home office is set up appropriately. This is vital for all workers, but may be especially important for tradies working from their home shed or garage, as the risks for both personal injury and property damage are much greater.

Your employees work environment, including entry and exit access, needs to comply with any workplace health and safety requirements specific to your business. This could include ergonomics, machine/electrical safety, fire access and first aid requirements.

You and your employee need to conduct a working from home safety audit and fix any non-compliances before you can allow your employee to work from home.

Youll also need to confirm exactly what your business insurance and workers compensation policies will cover, specifically: property and equipment; damage to the employees house or property; property damage caused by an employees motor vehicle being used for business purposes; and, if the employee will be inviting customers or other people, such as couriers, to their home, public liability insurance may be an issue.

With so many considerations, its best to contact your broker , agent or insurer to discuss how allowing employees to work from home will affect your policies, and to ask for any advice on how to ensure that your employee is working from their home in a safe and productive manner.

Holistic health considerations

One often-overlooked aspect of letting employees work from home is the effect on their mental health.

If an employee is working from home full-time, you wont be able to gauge their stress levels, job satisfaction or even determine if they are being bullied. As an employer, you may be responsible for any resulting psychological injuries under workers compensation.

One way to minimise this risk is to consider regular check-in days, where your employee comes into the office for a day for a scheduled debriefing another benefit of the three day/two day split. If this isnt possible, be sure to check in with them via other means.

Such check-in days also provide the employee with much-needed social interaction something that many workers dont value until its gone. Similarly, established communication media such as instant messaging and email can help any employees stay in touch with colleagues and work friends, and help them feel less isolated.

On this note, headsup.org.au provides useful information for employers and employees alike that can aid in creating a more mentally healthy, happy work environment both at home and at the office.
