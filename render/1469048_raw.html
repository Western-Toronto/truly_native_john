Photo Essay: Hammocks and Tree Dwelling

By Maptia , Co-founder Team Share
photo essay
inspiration
tweet Share stumble google

We hope you are still tucked up in bed with a good cup of coffee while you are reading this post, as t oday is World Sleep Day .

07:00 am - Tagahzout, Morocco. Outside the faded blue shutters of Maptia HQ, as the dawn colours wash the pale sky pink and we are still cocooned in that delightful semi-consciousness state before waking, we hear the local fishermen calling out to one another on the beach - setting out to make their early morning catch. Then a herd of goats come trotting along the narrow walkway beneath our windows - bleating and jostling their way past. It's low tide and if we peek outside we can see the wide expanse of wet sand catching the light of the sun as it rises. Someone is now brewing coffee in the kitchen, and the smells and sounds of the day draw us from our beds to the start of another beautiful day by the ocean in Morocco.

Where in the world are you waking up today?

Earlier this week, while pondering how best to enjoy this day, we did some thinking and came up with two equally relaxing solutions - hammocks and tree houses. Hammocks we have included for many reasons, not least because whether you believe it or not, you sleep better in a hammock .And as for the tree dwelling part, we feel that they are definitely in the same dreamy category and are quite simply, often like something out of a fairytale. They certainly look like somewhere there are adventures waiting to be had.

Ella, our lovely intern, has found 20 beautiful photographs of hammocks and tree houses for today's sleep-inspired photo essay and written some thoughtful captions to accompany them. We think that many of the photographs convey a kind of timeless tranquility. Thanks to all the charming photographers for sharing their beautiful images.

1 | Houalou, Nord, New Caledonia by Helen Evans

If you didnt already know, New Caledonia (a heavenly archipelago in the Pacific Ocean) has the richest biodiversity in the world per square kilometer. An intriguing place in many respects, this is somewhere that has been on our list of places to visit for a very long time.

2 | Location unknown by Grigory Gorbushin

We like optimism and we definitely like hammocks - the two seem to go hand in hand. Though were not entirely sure where this place is, we can recognise it as a mighty good place to hang your hammock.

3 | Vienna by Valentin Kouba

A personal favourite in this collection, we greatly approve of this guy's hammock technique. It seems appropriate that the photo was taken in the City of Dreams - reminding us that sometimes all you can do is surrender, and sleep.

4 | River Kwai near Kanchanaburi, Thailand by Mark Fischer

Floating downstream... this looks like an ideal place to rock gently from side to side while doing a spot of life meditation.

5 | Location unknown by Maria Safronova

There is something about hammocks that enable us to momentarily deny reality. We love the dream-like feeling of this photograph.

6 | Italy by Cleo Glover

You cant really go wrong with Italy and a hammock. Definitely an excuse to daydream and 35mm film just adds to the tranquil feeling of this capture.

7 | Ohanapecosh, Washington by Jackie Watanabe

Hammocks and halcyon.We tend to forget about needing to retreat and relax, and there is no better way to sleep than in a hammock - no, really .

('Halcyon' denotes a time in the past that was idyllically happy and peaceful.)

8 | Thottada Beach, Kerala, India by Rahul K. Thomas

We hear that Kerala in India is absolutely stunning. The geography of the state ranges from rugged mountainous to coastal lowlands - perfect hammock country, especially when the sun goes down.

9 | Brazil by Andy Farrer

This was taken somewhere in the huge expanse of ecological wetland that is the Pantanal - extending out of Brazil and all the way into Bolivia and Paraguay.

10 | Fort Greene Park, Brooklyn by Missy S

Trees are rarely the perfect distance apart for ones hammock, and so we are really rather jealous of this individual. Sun, shade, green grass, and a good read.

11 | Sri Lanka by Julian John

Probably more tree than it is house, this remarkable dwelling is located somewhere in the country known as The Pearl of the Indian Ocean, so called because of its incredible biodiversity and natural beauty.

12 | Crossville, Tennessee by Caitlyn Marler

Horace Burgess treehouse took 14 years to complete, built around a central oak tree 12 ft in diameter with 6 other trees acting as supporting pillars. It is held together by 258,000 nails and stands at nearly 100 ft tall. Clearly, if youre going to build a treehouse, make it a good one.

13 | Longwood Gardens, Pennsylvania by Mary Haber

Maybe its a leftover childhood dream, but there is something magical about the idea of having a hideaway in the trees... amongst the branches the real world tends to fall away.

14 | Osnabrck, Germany by Alasdair Jardine

This baumhuse in northwest Germany is simply stunning to look at - after all, why build a house on the ground when you can build one in the trees! Were sure there are many more architectural successes hidden away in trees all over the world.

15 | HapukuLodge in Kaikoura, New Zealand

These amazing tree houses in Kaikoura - designed to blend in to the natural landscape - boast views of mountains and Pacific coastline, and we can think of nothing better than being nestled 30 ft up.

16 | Cave Junction, Oregon by Emma Louise

Forest living. With the light filtering through the trees and not another soul in sight.

17 | Japan by Jack Shepherdson-Cullen

Home is wherever your treehouse is. At least that's what we reckon, and some just look deceptively easy to construct - all you need is a suitable source of wood, a hammer and a few nails and youre pretty much there.

18 | Location unknown by Joanna Eden

There is something really quite reassuring about tree houses. If youve ever feared that the relationship between nature and man is breaking down, then you only need to find a suitable forest dwelling and your fears will surely disappear.

19 | Wisconsin by Eric Schmuttenmaer

This tree house brings with it a rather sad story. Built in the summer of 1998, it was burnt down by some heartless folk shortly after this photo was taken in the February of 1999. However, were glad that this beautiful and unusual structure has been immortalised in this nostalgia-inducing photograph.

20 | Korowai Tribe Tree Houses in Southeastern Papua

Surely this is the tree house to end all tree houses. These incredible constructions by the Korowai Tribe can reach an unbelievable 114 ft tall, and though they come with an obvious fire risk and typically only last around 5 years, wed definitely be up for attempting a similar thing.
