Autism Symptom Severity Predicts Placebo Response

  • save saved

  • by Salynn Boyles
    Contributing Writer, MedPage Today

Action Points

  • This secondary analysis of the placebo arm of a randomized, controlled trial demonstrated that children and adolescents with more severe autism spectrum symptoms were less likely to show a placebo response.
  • Be aware that the trial this data was drawn from, which examined citalopram versus placebo for the treatment of repetitive behavior in children with ASD, demonstrated no difference between the drug and placebo.

Newly identified baseline predictors of placebo response in children and teens with autism spectrum disorders could help researchers recognize the drugs that work best for treating symptoms, researchers said.

In a secondary analysis of data from the STAART citalopram (Celexa) trial, higher scores on tests measuring severity of disruptive behaviors (primarily hyperactivity), mood and symptom burden, and caregiver strain were associated with lower placebo responses, reported Bryan H. King, MD , of Seattle Children's Hospital, and colleagues.

Specifically, participants in the placebo group were less likely to respond if they entered the study with scores exceeding the median for disruptive behavior (RR 2.0, 95% CI 1.0-4.0), mood or autism symptoms (RR 2.5, 95% CI 1.2-5.2), or caregiver strain (RR 2.5, 95% CI 1.2-5.3), they wrote in JAMA Pediatrics .

"The power of future trials may be significantly enhanced by taking these factors into account, and studies designed to attempt to replicate these findings should be a priority," the authors noted.

The STAART ( Studies to Advance Autism Research and Treatment ) trial compared citalopram hydrobromide, a selective serotonin reuptake inhibitor (SSRI), versus placebo for the treatment of repetitive behavior in children with autism spectrum disorders. King's group conducted STAART and found that "there were just as many responders to placebo as to citalopram."

There are currently no drugs approved specifically for the core and secondary symptoms associated with autism, including self-injury, aggression, hyperactivity, and repetitive behaviors. But many different classes of drugs are used and have been evaluated for the treatment of these symptoms in clinical trials.

A large placebo response -- ranging from 20% to 50% -- in trials involving children and teens on the autism spectrum has made it difficult to determine which drugs actually work and which ones don't, the researchers noted.

"One consequence of a higher-than-expected placebo response in a trial is to increase the chances of a signal being lost owing to lack of statistical power, resulting in a potentially useful treatment being passed over or discarded," they wrote.

King and colleagues conducted a secondary analysis of data from their previously published study that included 149 children and teens (ages 5 to 17) on the autism spectrum randomly assigned to treatment with either citalopram or placebo for 12 weeks.

The goal of the new analysis was to determine the baseline characteristics that influenced response to placebo or the active drug at week 12.

A positive response was defined as having a score of at least "much improved" on the Clinical Global Impression-Improvement scale at week 12. Baseline measures included demographic (sex, age, weight, and pubertal status), clinical, and family measures.

Clinical variables included baseline illness severity (using the Aberrant Behavior Checklist, the Child and Adolescent Symptom Inventory, the Vineland Adaptive Behavior Scales, the Repetitive Behavior Scale-Revised, and the Children's Yale-Brown Obsessive-Compulsive Scale). Family stress was measured using the Caregiver Strain Questionnaire.

The analysis revealed that of study participants scoring in the lower half of the Disruptive Behavior, Mood/Autism, and Caregiver Strain measures, 50% responded to treatment with placebo at 12 weeks compared with 30% of those treated with citalopram.

The placebo group was significantly less likely than those treated with citalopram to respond at week 12 if they entered the study more symptomatic on each of the three composite measures.

They were also at least two times less likely to respond to treatment.

In the initial trial analysis, no benefit was shown for citalopram (34% response rate) relative to placebo (32% response rate) with regard to the primary and secondary outcome measures examined.

When the researchers investigated whether those in the citalopram group who scored in the upper half of the Caregiver Strain composite had different outcomes from those in the placebo group with similar Caregiver Strain scores, they again failed to find a signal for citalopram.

"This finding may indicate that the global improvement that was identified in this subpopulation was unlikely to have been associated with a distinct change in repetitive behaviors and may have been due to changes that were not adequately captured in our rating (e.g., mood or anxiety symptoms)," the researchers wrote.

They added that the fact that SSRIs are so widely prescribed for autism may suggest that clinicians and families identify benefits in areas other than repetitive behavior.

In an accompanying editorial, L. Eugene Arnold, MD , of Ohio State University in Columbus, congratulated the researchers "for proving that a failed trial does not have to be a useless or wasted trial."

He noted that the finding that patients with fewer symptoms and caregiver stress had the highest response rates to placebo treatment suggests that citalopram interfered with the placebo response in these patients.

"[King and colleagues] naturally suspected adverse effects as the explanation, but further analysis failed to uphold that hypothesis," Arnold wrote. "It is still possible that something else in the effect of the active drug interfered with placebo response. One possibility could be that the intended therapeutic increase in serotonin neurotransmission is counterproductive in those with low severity."

He added that the "triple interaction" of initial symptom severity, treatment, and response rate identifies initial severity as a moderator of the placebo, but not the active drug, effect.

"Of course, this requires thinking of placebo as a treatment, which is a reasonable perspective too often ignored," he wrote. "If we assume placebo to be the natural response state, it might be more technically accurate to say that citalopram moderates the effect of initial severity on placebo response, increasing the response of those with high severity and decreasing the response of those with low severity, as King et al. suggest."

The authors pointed out that "the results of our secondary analysis do not change the finding that citalopram appeared not to be specifically advantageous compared with placebo for the treatment of repetitive behaviors. However, this analysis ... highlights the ongoing need for placebo-controlled trials of medications commonly used for children with autism spectrum disorders to determine whether the benefits of specific drugs substantially outweigh their risks."

The study was funded by the National Institute of Mental Health (NIMH), the Eunice Kennedy Shriver National Institute of Child Health and Human Development (NICHD), the National Institute of Neurological Disorders and Stroke (NINDS), the National Institute on Deafness and Other Communication Disorders, and the National Institute of Environmental Health Sciences.

One co-author reported receiving research funding from NIMH, the Eunice Kennedy Shriver NICHD, Autism Speaks, the Foundation of Hope, Bristol Myers-Squibb, Curemark, Merck, Forest, Otsuka Research Institute, SynapDx, and Seaside Pharmaceuticals, as well as serving as a consultant to sanofi aventis and ABT Associates.

Arnold reported various financial relationships (research funding, advisory board, consulting, travel support) with CureMark, Forest, Lilly, Shire, Biomarin, Novartis, Noven, Roche, Seaside Therapeutics, and TPharma.
