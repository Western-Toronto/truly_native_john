The IP headache of 3D printing

Written by Emma Jones
Copyright , Design , Patents , Trademark Leave a Comment

Not so long ago, it all seemed too futuristic to be possible, and yet, barely a week goes by without one 3D printing story or another hitting the headlines. Already adopted by car manufacturers , shoe designers , artists and space agencies , the technology has started to become accessible to the average Joe with machines going on sale this week at Home Depot (UK retailer Maplin made a similar move last month) . That should mean those responsible for keeping IP legislation up to date have had plenty of time to get prepared. Er, thats right, isnt it guys?

Not according to Bay Oak Laws Daniel Richardson who takes it as read that : Just as IP law was ill-prepared for the digital media revolution in the late 1990s, current [US] laws, like the Digital Millennium Copyright Act (DMCA), do not adequately address the challenges 3D printing will present. He goes on to predict that at some point, Congress will address 3D printings impact on IP, possibly by passing laws prohibiting 3D scanning of copyrighted objects, until then, however, companies could protect their IP by using digital rights management software that prevents CAD files from being copied or only allows them to work on certain machines.

3D printers may be the next big challenge to the protection of IP rights agrees Margaret Ryan from Phillips Ormonde Fitzpatrick, before recalling photocopiers were an early challenge, and adding: The digital age has made things much more difficult for IP owners, with the ease with which music, films and written works can be copied and distributed. 3D printers go a step further, [they] put the tools in the hands of individuals to manufacture products and provide a new way for commercial organisations to copy products.

It is the potential rise in counterfeit activity that is also of concern to Finnegans Elizabeth D Ferrill, P Andrew Riley and Benjamin T Sirolly in their article on the implications of 3D printing for car and parts manufacturers: 3D printers shift IP considerations by making infringement happen not only faster but much harder to find. [It] accelerates the time it takes for infringing products to enter the market. For example, a knockoff manufacturer could start making a part before it hits the stores by using a 3D scanner to capture a new car design at a prerelease event like a trade show. [] 3D printing also may exponentially increase the number of potential infringers, thus complicating the task of locating the source of infringing products.

Over at IPKat, contributor Neil Wilkof is interested in the dip in tax revenue that he expects once3D printing turnsevery home into a potential workplace, explaining: Within the confines of the home, [the product] will be made and then consumed, all without generating any taxable revenue. As such, there will be even fewer tradable goods [] both within and between countries. If so, and on one view of 3D printing, the greater the success of 3D printing at the private level, the potentially greater is the harm to the legitimate public coffers.

Bird & Birds Hilary Atherton is most interested in the implications for the fashion sector: Exemptions from some types of IP infringement for personal use, combined with the practical and PR difficulties of pursuing individual consumers, mean that fashion brands are likely to seek to enforce their rights against those higher up the fashion food chain. The fashion industry may look to adopt the strategies of the already disrupted music and film industries, obtaining blocking injunctions requiring ISPs to prevent access to sites offering infringing design files from which 3D prints can be made. [] Online, those brands are looking at creating their own applications for 3D printing to stay ahead, possibly by offering a guarantee of quality through the licensing of design files made available to consumers in online libraries similar to iTunes, and by offering increased personalisation through customisation.

In its white paper, Mayer Browns Peter Dickinson emphasises the benefits as well as the risks: The opportunities are clear to see: speed to market, on-demand production and consequent economic growth in those countries from which creative and innovative engineering solutions originate. But the challenges need careful consideration also. The new world IP risks are enormous and the boundaries of multi-jurisdictional IP law are yet to be tested by what this technology has to offer.

Most commentators expect copyright to be the site of the first major 3D printing-generated IP battle, but in its newsletter Burns & Levinsons Mark Schonfeld points out: 3D printing is already the subject of patent litigation: Stratasys, a leading manufacturer of 3D printers, has sued Afinia, a popular 3D printer, for patent infringement; 3D Systems has sued Envision Tec and Formlabs for patent infringement. And, he expects patent-driven infringement cases to escalate: The USPTO has received more than 6,800 patent applications related to 3D printing technology, and legal experts expect that there will be many battles in the future between 3D printing companies.

Its not just battles between manufacturers that are relevant here points out PatentlyO guest blogger Lucas Osborn : Much as music and movie industries had to battle digital copyright infringement, patent owners may be on the cusp of their own digital patent war. People can create, modify, and transmit 3D CAD files just as easily as they can .mp3 files. Coupled with the internet, 3D printing technology decentralises and largely anonymises the manufacture of tangible objects. If the physical object is only a click away from being produced by a persons 3D printer, should a court hold that the digital file infringes a patent claim to the physical object, either directly or under the doctrine of equivalents? If not, will claims of indirect infringement be an effective tool against the individuals and websites that host and transmit the CAD files? These questions will be of increasing importance in an era of ubiquitous 3D printing technology.
