Multiple Sclerosis: Understanding Your Symptoms

Living Easier With MS, One Life Hack at a Time

  • By Denise Mann
  • Reviewed by Farrokh Sohrabi, MD

Having multiple sclerosis can zap your energy. Discover daily lifestyle tactics to help with this and more.

Expert Advice for MS Symptoms

Living with multiple sclerosis (MS) can be challenging, but some simple lifestyle techniques can help you overcome these issues and improve your quality of life. Just ask Stephanie Butler, 25, a Northern Virginia-based nurse who has MS and created the website " Just Keep S'Myelin ," where she blogs and shares practical ideas what she calls life hacks that others with MS can use.

MS symptoms ebb and flow, ranging from mild to severe, and may include fatigue , balance issues, bladder problems, brain fog, and trouble walking. Here are some ideas from Butler and others to help manage them.

Energy-Zapping Fatigue

Running errands is strenuous, especially when Im not feeling well, Butler says. Using grocery-delivery services or ordering what I need in advance and simply picking it up instead of walking around the store make a big difference.

Another tip: Power naps can help you recharge before an important event, says Karen Blitz, DO, director of the North Shore-Long Island Jewish Multiple Sclerosis Center in East Meadow, New York.

Gait and Balance Issues

Trouble with balance may leave you unable to walk without assistance. If you have trouble getting around, canes are helpful, Butler says. Walkers with seats are great because you dont need to look for a chair if you need a rest.

Scooters are another option, Dr. Blitz says. They do double duty by helping you get around while conserving energy. If you have difficulty walking , you can scoot or you can scoot to your location, and then walk once you get there, Blitz says.

Brain Fog

About 50 percent of people with MS will develop problems with cognition, according to the National Multiple Sclerosis Society (NMSS). Brain-training apps can help you stay sharp, Butler says. Blitz agrees: Cognitive games are good for MS and can be a nice way to tax your brain, whether you play on a smartphone or computer, or play Sudoku in a book.

Making lists is key for Brittni Castilaw, a 26-year-old marketing coordinator from Houston who was diagnosed with MS about two years ago and who also blogs about it. Being forgetful is a day-to-day struggle, she says. I'd be lost without the Wunderlist app on my mobile phone. From grocery lists to tasks, this is my go-to life hack every day.

Overheating

Many people with MS experience symptoms such as numbness or a tremor when they get overheated, whether from the temperature or during exercise. This can also occur when you have a fever.

To help stay cool, drink cold water to cool off from the inside out, Butler says. I always bring a cooling towel to put behind my neck at the gym or when I'm going anywhere outside during the summer, she adds. Another trick: I stick some grapes in the freezer and snack on them throughout the day. It's a great and delicious way to stay cool!"

She also makes her own cooling packs. Just combine 3 cups of water with 1 cup of rubbing alcohol in a re-sealable plastic bag and stick it in the freezer. The mixture gets freezing cold but doesn't freeze solid, resulting in a slush that stays cold for a couple of hours.

Castilaw says she uses cooling towels, which are easy to find and buy online, to help prevent overheating. Its always hot in Houston, so I wear these all the time, she says.

Depressed Mood

It's easy to get down in the dumps with MS," Castilaw says. But she believes the key to living a better life is decidingto have a positive outlook.

Butler also tries to put a positive spin on any setbacks she may have because of MS. I watch movies, read a book that I've been meaning to get around to, and I write, she says. "Writing is a great outlet for me, and the pieces that I produce during a relapse are often the ones that connect the most to my readers."

A new pet has also been a source of comfort for Butler. Last year I had several relapses, and as a result I was a little down," she recounts. "My fianc and I decided to get a kitten, and honestly she has brought so much joy into my life! She makes me laugh every single day, and having to care for her took my mind off of myself a bit."

Blitz adds that exercise can help you beat the blues by boosting levels of the brain's natural feel-good chemicals. Everyone with MS can do something, as long as you keep cool while doing it, she says. Start slowly, and consider working with a physical therapist to develop a plan.

For some people, however, treatment to address depression may be necessary, Blitz says.

Bladder Control Issues

At least 80 percent of people with MS will experience bladder control problems like frequency and urgency, according to the NMSS.

The best thing for me is to scout out the bathrooms as soon as I arrive at a destination," Butler says. Knowing where they are puts my mind at ease a bit because when I have to go, I have to go!

She also suggests going to the bathroom whenever you have the chance. I never pass up a good clean bathroom while I'm out and about. My fianc and I have taken to calling these 'op-pee-tunities' because if you aren't laughing, you're crying.

A little planning also helps, Blitz says. If you know you have bladder issues, pre-plan your bathroom stops, she suggests. If you have to go urgently every three hours, make a plan to go every two hours." Also, dont drink coffee before going out because caffeine can irritate your bladder and worsen symptoms.

Dexterity Issues

My biggest issues have been foot drop, and weakness in my right hand my dominant hand, of course," Butler says. Aside from my cane, I have a pair of really sturdy hiking boots that come up to the ankle they help keep mytoes from dropping down because of their thick soles."

Dexterity kits are also a boon. "These help me grip everything such as toothbrushes, door knobs, utensils, and pens whenmy grip is weak, she says.

Blitz notes that "a lot of adaptive equipment is available, but theres no one-size-fits-all solution." Work with an occupational therapist to find out which tools will be most helpful for you. These may include magnetic shirt buttons, slip-on shoes, corrective glasses, a long-handed scrubber for the shower, and many other assistive devices. Theres something to help address just about every MS symptom and lifestyle, she says.
