  • Health Cares Big Data Mandate

    Sharing information collected in clinical trials could lead to better treatments and dramatically reduce drug development costs.
    Linda Marsa Dec 16, 2013
  • Tweet

  • Can an anti-ulcer medication thats available on pharmacy shelves combat the most common form of lung cancer? Will we soon be using an old epilepsy drug to quell symptoms of inflammatory bowel disease, or could we enlist a little-used class of antidepressants to battle a particularly deadly type of lung disease for which we have few effective therapies? The answer is a qualified yes, if a series of Stanford University studies that are currently underway bear fruit. Scientists there have identified potential new uses for these old drugs by scanning thousands of gene profiles stored in publicly available databases.

    Pooling and analyzing vast amounts of information can reveal previously unseen patterns and trends that arent apparent in smaller studies. By systematically using a number-crunching algorithm that grouped illnesses by how they changed gene activity, and then matching them with medications that blocked these pathways, researchers came up with these seemingly quirky pairings of diseases and drugs which they are now testing on humans.

    But the larger lesson here is that this big-data approach could open the door to an entirely new method of unearthing beneficial therapeutics for a host of ills. Using data that already exist and that continue to be collected the process could be much swifter and far less costly than the current drug-development process. Unfortunately, researchers are still largely unable to tap a wealth of information that would accelerate drug discovery. Test results from clinical trials often aren't released by drug companies because of trade secrets or proprietary interests.

    Question:

    If you could scale up one remarkable health care idea, what would it be?

    Answer:

    " Make the big data were already collecting for clinical trials available to the public. It would enable better transparency in clinical recommendations. This can lead to better treatments and help lower overall health care costs ."

    - Atul Butte, M.D., Ph.D., chief of the Division of SystemsMedicine and associate professor of Pediatricsand Computer Science, Stanford University


    A major cost driver for our health care system is the price of drugs, and the biggest driver of those costs is the expense of clinical trials, says Atul Butte, M.D., Ph.D., who led the research teams at Stanford. Based on their findings, Dr. Butte explains that applying big-data tools to publicly available records is already cutting down the time and money needed to translate a laboratory finding into a successful drug treatment from a decade or more and $1 billion, to roughly one to two years and about $100,000. However, researchers need access to more data to continue this success. Butte makes it clear: If I had to promote one thing that would speed innovation, streamline costs for the development of life-saving treatments and spur major changes in medicine, it would be to make the results of clinical trials publicly available.

    According to numerous studies, the rule of thumb is that out of the every 5,000 to 10,000 compounds that look promising in the lab, only five make it to human tests, and only one of them eventually makes it through the rigorous Food and Drug Administration approval process for use on actual patients. Typically, to test the efficacy of new drugs or medical interventions, sometimes thousands of people volunteer to participate in clinical trials. Thats why it takes upwards of $1 billion a figure that includes the costs for these dry wells and a decade or more to bring a drug from the laboratory bench to a patients bedside.

    These tests routinely generate billions of data points of information on volunteers responses to therapeutics based on their medical history, age, gender, ethnicity and a host of other factors. These randomized tests, especially the gigantic mega-trials that encompass thousands of people, have the potential to transform medical practice. The big-data tools now available could convert the more than 300 billion points of molecular, clinical and epidemiological data, which have been measured by scientists and physicians over the past decade, into better diagnostics and therapeutics and provide us with new insights into disease. Unfortunately, because so many clinical trials fail, results often arent recorded.

    As a consequence, valuable information is lost that with big-data analysis could be used to make better treatment decisions and provide more reliable benchmarks about a products safety profile and efficacy. That lost information could also help identify subsets of patients that respond better to particular therapies, reduce unnecessary risks for study participants and illuminate potential new avenues of research, according to a 2013 New England Journal of Medicine report. Releasing data from completed studies would also speed innovation and increase efficiency of drug development because it would lead to better design of subsequent trials and reduce duplication of effort among competing companies, says the same NEJM study. Mandating the release of raw, clinical trials data with identifying details removed to preserve patient confidentiality would enhance transparency and provide us with a better understanding of human biomedicine, says Butte.

    Fortunately, in the past decade, there has been a growing momentum to expand public access to information collected from ongoing and completed clinical trials. The National Academy of Sciences Institute of Medicine is currently hammering out guidelines for responsible sharing of this data, and pharmaceutical associations in the United States and Europe have recently made commitments to communicate this information. In another step forward, major pharmaceutical corporations, such as Roche and GlaxoSmithKline, have adopted policies to allow researchers to access data from tests of their products that are already on the market. There are, however, still valuable data lost every year from other tests and trials. By making this information freely available to the research community for further analysis, we can maximize the benefits from human tests and potentially transform medical care for the better.
