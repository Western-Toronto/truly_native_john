Published on December 9th, 2013 | by Jessica Stillman

Experts predict the high-tech future of advertising


The future of advertising: from Mad Men to Math Men

Just about any post or article on the future of advertising and the evolution of the advertising industry these days references Mad Men , but you dont have to go back to the days of bullet bras and three-martini lunches to see how much the ad industry has changed. Do you remember what you did first thing in the morning ten years ago? What you did after dinner?

These days the answer to both these questions usually revolves around our gadgets ( 90% of young people sleep with their smartphones , after all). Look back a decade and your routine probably was based more on coffee and TV Guide than your tablet or iPhone. And just as consumers daily habits have radically altered thanks to technology, so too have the realities of reaching them for advertisers. Its a momentous shift thats only just begun, according to a chorus of experts.

So what will the future of advertising look like as these changes play out?

Mobile first. Screen agnostic.

If youre typical of the age, from the moment you wake up to the moment you close your eyes, your phone is with you, as it is from the instant it occurs to you you might like to make a purchase to the second you hand over your credit card. No wonder advertisers are racing towards a mobile first strategy. Bonin Bough, VP of Global Media and Consumer Engagement at Mondelz International (makers of snack foods like Oreos and Triscuits) and former head of digital strategy for Pepsi, recently told Bloomberg TV , that his firm is really focused on a mobile-first strategy. We made a commitment to spending 10% of our media on mobile marketing.

Why spend such a large chunk of a budget that runs into the billions on mobile? A mobile device is the only device you have with you throughout your day, he says, so its the ideal tool to help nudge you towards impulse purchases. Boughs team also found that simultaneously engaging consumers through social media on their mobile devices while they watch the companys TV spots at least doubles the effectiveness of those television ads.

Of course getting their messages out to all the many and various mobile devices that customers carry around is no picnic for advertisers, so Bob Lord, Global CEO of AOL Networks, predicts 2014 will be the year that marketers demand device agnostic advertising. New technologies are set to make this possible.

Innovations and technologies such as new targeting capabilities and new uses of data that empower marketers to gain a single view of the customer across all screens are leading this movement, because ultimately, a cross-screen strategy will drive the highest return for marketers, Lord writes.

Can ad agencies go automated?

With the industry going through such tech-driven tumult, the role of agencies is going to have to change. Technologists, creative art directors, and content producers can no longer operate in separate silos, Lord also predicts, adding with more consumer data being generated than ever before, marketing, data, and technology must be aligned.

Not everyone is optimistic about agencies ability to adapt. Big internet firms like Google and Facebook can provide a wealth of data about their vast audiences: this is encouraging many advertisers to deal with them directly to book slots, rather than going through a traditional ad agencys media-buying division, notes The Economist is a recent writeup ofthe Omnicom and Publicis mergerand the changes rocking the industry.

All these developments make the future role of the advertising agency a lot murkier, the article continues, though it concedes that real-time bidding is still a small part of the online-advertising businessaround 19% of online display ads in America are now bought and sold in this way. By 2017 it may reach 29%, however. The conclusion: To stay relevant, the agencies will need to transform themselves into advertising platforms this means providing clever technology to their clients to automate the buying process. The problem is that tech companies may prove much better at providing this.

The Math Men, the article warns, may end up getting the better of the Mad Men.

Whether or not theyre optimistic about agencies evolving, nearly everyone agrees a tsunami of change is coming, and, as Lord puts it, automated, or programmatic, advertising technologies are set to replace unwieldy manual media planning and buying processes that eat up too much time and overlook critical consumer data.
