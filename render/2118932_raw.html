Then + Now :: Holiday Card History + Traditions

Posted Monday, December 9th, 2013 by Nicolle Mackinnon, Red Stamp Community & filed under Holidays , Surprises .

Theres something festive in the air around the holidays that makes me want to pick up the phone and have long chats with my friends and relatives who live outside my everyday radius. Maybe its Santa in the mall, the chill in the air, or the twinkling lights, making the dark, early-morning skies more loveable.

Whatever it is, Christmas makes me antsy to reconnect with all my loved ones, near and far. Unfortunately, in-person connection isnt always possible {especially now that Im married and were splitting our holiday time between families}. Thats why I turn to Christmas cards

Today is Christmas Card Day , a little-known holiday that honors Sir Henry Cole, the creator of the first commercialized Christmas card. In Great Britain in 1843, he had the brilliant idea to partner with his illustrator friend, John Horsley, to create holiday-themed cards to send to friends and family for Christmas. Instead of handwriting long letters to keep relatives up to date on the local happenings, Cole thought hed capitalize on the already established popularity of all-purpose greeting cards and help people save time with cards that reflected holiday traditions.

The early American Christmas cards depicted carolers, families eating feasts together, cute animals and Santa sipping carbonated beverages . Handwritten messages told of current events, like the struggles of the Depression, and were used to catch up with distant family members.Thats my favorite part of this Christmas-card history :: even at the start of the card-sending phenomenon, senders were using cards to reconnect, just like I do now!

The difference? Today, we have so many more OPTIONS! From postcards to e-cards, from text cards to handwritten cards, picking out a design is a struggle enough, let alone trying to navigate the etiquette that goes with card-sending.

To help you (and myself!) this holiday, Ive solicited the help of a few seasoned etiquette professionals.

If youre sending cards through the mail, pre-printed addresses on Avery office labels must be avoided at all costs, says Kelley Lilien, etiquette blogger and influential tastemaker known as Mrs. Lilien. Dont do it, its goesh . {RS Advice :: print directly on the envelope or take this time to practice your penmanship.}

Another big question :: when should you send your cards? Erin Loechner, Executive Editor at Clementine Daily , says its better to send them late than never at all. Christmas is a time to show your loved ones how much you care about them, so carve out some time to write a few heartfelt notes to those closest to you, she says. Even if mid-January rolls around and youve got a stack of correspondence you havent yet tackled, do it! Im not above sending good cheer a few weeks {months?} later than anticipated. {RS Advice :: better yet, send New Years or Valentines Day cards!}

Mrs. Lilien and Erin also said theyve been seeing a trend towards email and text cards. If you think the electronic route is more your style, make sure to personalize each card by including the recipients name and a personal message, says Marilyn Pentel, creator and owner of Mannerly Manners . It lets them know that theyre special to you.

Since were all now privy on how to send our cards, whats the hottest in card design in 2013? Michigan-based graphic designer Anne Pageau says, There is definitely a surge of calligraphy and hand-lettering in the stationery world right now. The chalkboard look [is popular], even in print and digital, thanks to Dana Tanamachi , no doubt!

Judith Kostroski, prop stylist for Style-Architects in Minneapolis, says shes noticed a lot of clean lines and pops of color in cards this year. She also said, Ive seen some really interesting black background cards with a pop of white or gold text with graphics or a simple white background with bolder colors. Handwriting fonts are also really popular because, she says, they give a sense of a vintage chic flair and a personalized touch!
