            </em>

         <tr>
          <td align="left" class="sustainableLeftCol" valign="top" width="50%">
           <p>
            At FINNE Architects, we have been practicing sustainable design for over twenty years. We are members of the Cascadia Green Building Council and we are LEED certified. Every project is designed to fit to the site in a sensitive manner, to conserve resources by using passive heating and cooling, to maximize the use of natural light, and to employ sustainable building materials. We also promote a philosophy of building smaller homes, but with a focus on quality, rather than large homes with no real character. Smaller homes are more sustainable, and we also promote local suppliers of major building materials such as window, siding, roofing, etc. Another important part of our practice is the attention to detailing and craftsmanship in our projects. Our passion for craft and detailing results in construction that is extremely durable and materials are protected for a long life-cycle.
           </p>
          </td>
          <td align="left" class="sustainableRightCol" valign="top" width="50%">
           <p>
            All of our projects use passive ideas such as highly insulated building envelopes, thermal mass floors, large roof overhangs and large amounts of natural light and ventilation. We also use active systems such as drilled wells for groundsource heat exchangers, hydronic solar panels for domestic hot water, and photo voltaic panels for electricity pro duction.
           </p>
           <p>
            Sustainable design is not simply creating a laundry-list of green materials; rather, it is creating enduring building form and construc- tion with appropriate long life-cycle, low-energy consuming materials.
           </p>
          </td>
         </tr>

         <tr>
          <td align="left" class="sustainableLeftCol" valign="top">
           <div>
            <h2>
             Site Strategies
            </h2>
            <ul>
             <li>
              Solar Orientation to capture maximum passive solar heating
             </li>
             <li>
              Wind Orientation to capture prevailing breezes
             </li>
             <li>
              North-facing glass minimized to limit heat loss
             </li>
             <li>
              Careful site study to minimize disturbance to native  vegetation or other sensitive areas
             </li>
            </ul>
           </div>
          </td>
          <td align="left" class="sustainableRightCol" valign="top">
           <div>
            <h2>
             Building Form
            </h2>
            <ul>
             <li>
              Deep overhangs provide solar shading and pro- tect exterior materials, giving longer life cycle for exterior materials
             </li>
             <li>
              Thin building footprint designed to maximize natrual light and natural cross-ventilation
             </li>
            </ul>
           </div>
          </td>
         </tr>

          <td align="left" class="sustainableLeftCol" valign="top">
           <div>
            <h2>
             Insulation
            </h2>
            <ul>
             <li>
              2x8 wall construction to achieve 42% higher insulation value than standard 2x6 walls
             </li>
             <li>
              Some projects have walls up to 12 thick for even greater insulation value
             </li>
             <li>
              Roof insulation cavities up to 14 thick
             </li>
             <li>
              Use cellulose blown-in insulation, made from re-cycled paper, instead of fiberglass batt
             </li>
             <li>
              Use closed cell spray foam insulation, for tight 
				  				air sealing and high R-value
             </li>
            </ul>
           </div>
          </td>
          <td align="left" class="sustainableRightCol" valign="top">
           <div>
            <h2>
             Rainwater
            </h2>
            <ul>
             <li>
              Roof drainage used for landscape irrigation
             </li>
             <li>
              Roof drainage directed back to soil so as to minimize contribution to storm sewer systems
             </li>
             <li>
              Roof drainage harvested to use as gray water within the house
             </li>
            </ul>
           </div>
          </td>
